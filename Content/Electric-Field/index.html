    Electric Field

    The Electric Field is a vector field that describes the influence a charge exerts on other charges in its surroundings. It represents the force per unit charge that a small positive test charge would experience at a given point in space.

    Symbols

    • $\vec E\quad$ Electric Field (unit: Newton per Coulomb $\left(\frac{N}{C}\right)$ or Volts per meter $\left(\frac{V}{M}\right)$ )
    • $\vec F\quad$ Electrostatic Force acting on a test charge (unit: Newton, N)
    • $q\quad$ Electric Charge (unit: Coulomb, C)
    • $k_e\quad$ Coulomb's Constant ( $k_e=\cfrac{1}{4\pi e_0}\simeq8.99\times10^9\;\cfrac{N\cdot{m^2}}{C^2}$)
    • $\epsilon_0\quad$ is the vacuum permittivity ($\epsilon_0\simeq8,854\cdot10^{-12}\;\frac{C^2}{N\cdot m^2}$)

    Formulas

    • Definition from Force:
      The electric field at a point is defined as the force per unit charge: $$\vec{E}=\cfrac{\vec{F}}{q}$$
    • Electric Field Due to a Point Charge:
      Mathematical expression: $$\vec E=k_e\cfrac{q}{r^2}\hat r$$

    Unit

    • SI Unit: Is the Newton per Coulombs: $$\vec E=\cfrac{N}{C}$$

    Properties

    • Superposition Principle:
      The total electric field at a point is the vector sum of the fields created by all individual charges: $$\vec E_{tot}=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}\vec{E_i}$$

    • Vector Nature:
      The electric field has both magnitude and direction. The direction is radially outward for a positive charge and radially inward for a negative charge.

    • Field Lines Representation:
      Electric field lines originate from positive charges and terminate at negative charges. Their density indicates the field's strength.

    • Relation to Potential:
      The electric field is related to the electric potential by: $$\vec E=-\nabla V$$

      • $\nabla V\quad$ (called the gradient of V) represents the rate of change of potential with respect to position.
