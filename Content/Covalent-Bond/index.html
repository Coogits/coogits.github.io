    Covalent Bond

    A covalent bond is a type of chemical bond formed when two atoms share one or more pairs of electrons in order to achieve a full outer electron shell, typically in non-metals.

    Characteristics

    • Strong Chemical Bond:
      the covalent bond is considered a strong chemical bond.
      • It involves the sharing of electrons between atoms, which creates a stable electron configuration.
      • The electrostatic attraction between the positively charged nuclei and the shared electrons holds the atoms tightly together.

    Process

    • Formation:
      • Atoms share electrons to fill their outermost energy levels, achieving greater stability.
      • Each shared pair of electrons constitutes one covalent bond.
    • Characteristics:
      • Covalent bonds can form between atoms of the same element (e.g., H₂, O₂) or different elements (e.g., H₂O).
      • The strength of a covalent bond depends on the number of electron pairs shared: single, double, or triple bonds.

    ### Types of Covalent Bonds

    By Quantity of Shared Electrons

    1. Single Covalent Bond
    • In a single covalent bond, two atoms share one pair of electrons.
    • It is the most common type of bond and is typically formed between atoms of non-metals.
    • Example:
      In hydrogen (H₂), each hydrogen atom shares one electron, forming a single covalent bond: $$H-H$$
    2. Double Covalent Bond
    • A double covalent bond involves the sharing of two pairs of electrons between two atoms.
    • This bond is stronger than a single covalent bond.
    • Example:
      In oxygen (O₂), two oxygen atoms share two pairs of electrons: $$O=O$$
    3. Triple Covalent Bond
    • A triple covalent bond occurs when three pairs of electrons are shared between two atoms.
    • It is even stronger than a double bond. Example:
      In nitrogen (N₂), two nitrogen atoms share three pairs of electrons: $$N\equiv N$$

    By Electronegativity Difference

    Polar Covalent Bond
    • A polar covalent bond forms when two atoms share electrons unequally, resulting in a slight electrical dipole. One atom attracts the shared electrons more strongly due to its higher electronegativity.
    • The atom with the greater electronegativity becomes slightly negative, and the other becomes slightly positive.
    • Example:
      In water (H₂O), oxygen is more electronegative than hydrogen, so the electrons are more attracted to oxygen, making it slightly negative and hydrogen slightly positive.
    Nonpolar Covalent Bond
    • A nonpolar covalent bond occurs when two atoms share electrons equally. This typically happens between atoms of the same element or when the electronegativities of the two atoms are very similar.
    • Example:
      In oxygen (O₂), both oxygen atoms have the same electronegativity, so they share the electrons equally: $$O=O$$
