    Extensive and Intensive Quantity

    In thermodynamics and physical sciences, properties of a system are often classified as either extensive or intensive. Here are the definitions:

    Definitions

    • Extensive Quantity:
      An extensive quantity is a property that depends on the amount of matter or the size of the system. If you combine two identical systems, the extensive property will double (or add up accordingly).
    • Intensive Quantity:
      An intensive quantity is a property that does not depend on the size or the amount of matter in the system. Intensive properties remain the same regardless of how much of the substance is present.

    Properties

    • Extensive Quantity:
      Extensive properties are additive. For two non-interacting systems A and B, the total extensive property X is given by: $$X_{tot}=X_A+X_B$$
    • Intensive Quantity:
      Intensive properties are invariant under scaling. They do not change if you combine or split systems that are identical.

    Examples

    • Extensive Quantity:
      • Mass: Doubling the amount of material doubles the mass.
      • Volume: A larger system occupies more space.
      • Internal Energy: The total energy stored in a system increases with the number of particles.
      • Heat Capacity: The total heat capacity increases with the amount of substance.
    • Intensive Quantities:
      • Temperature: A substance's temperature does not change if you divide it into parts.
      • Pressure: In a uniform system, the pressure is the same throughout, independent of the systemâ€™s size.
      • Density (Physics): Although mass and volume are extensive, density (mass per unit volume) is intensive.
