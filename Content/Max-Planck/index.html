    Max Planck

    Max Planck was a German theoretical physicist who is best known for his foundational work in the development of quantum theory.
    His work revolutionized our understanding of energy, light, and matter at microscopic scales.

    • Born: 23-04-1858
    • Died: 04-10-1947

    Key Contributions

    Planck's Quantum Hypothesis

    • Age: 1900
      Planck is most famous for introducing the idea that energy is not emitted or absorbed continuously, but rather in discrete quantities or "quanta." This concept was pivotal in the development of quantum mechanics.

    Planck's Constant

    • Planck's constant:
      He introduced a fundamental constant, now known as Planck's constant (h), which relates the energy of a quantum to its frequency. The equation is: $$E=h\cdot f$$

      where

      • $E$ is Energy (Physics)
      • $h$ is Planck's constant
      • $f$ is Frequency of the radiation

    Planck's Law

    He formulated Planck's Law of Black-Body Radiation, which described how objects emit radiation at different wavelengths depending on their temperature.
    This law solved the "ultraviolet catastrophe" predicted by classical physics and laid the groundwork for quantum mechanics.

    Foundation for Quantum Mechanics

    Planck's work laid the foundation for quantum mechanics, influencing other great physicists like Albert Einstein, Niels Bohr, and Werner Heisenberg.
    Though Planck himself was initially reluctant to embrace the full implications of quantum theory, his work helped explain phenomena that could not be explained by classical physics.

    Planck Length, Time, and Temperature

    In theoretical physics, there are also units named after Planck, such as Planck length (the smallest measurable length), Planck time (the smallest measurable time interval), and Planck temperature (the highest theoretically possible temperature).
    These units emerge from the combination of Planck's constant, the speed of light, and gravitational constants, representing fundamental limits to our understanding of the universe.

    Curiosity

    • A Reluctant Revolutionary:
      Interestingly, Planck did not fully embrace the implications of his own theory.
      A classical physicist at heart, he introduced quantization as a mathematical trick rather than a deep physical truth.
      It was only later, through the work of scientists like Einstein and Bohr, that the quantum theory expanded into a full framework for understanding atomic and subatomic behavior.
