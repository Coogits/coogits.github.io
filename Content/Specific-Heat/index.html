    Specific Heat

    Specific heat capacity (or simply specific heat) is the amount of heat Q required to change the temperature of one unit mass of a substance by one unit of temperature.

    Formulas

    • Specific Heat: It is mathematically expressed as: $$c=\cfrac{Q}{m\Delta T}$$
    • Molar Specific Heat: is the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of one mole of a substance by one kelvin (or one degree Celsius).

    Symbols

    • $c\quad$ Specific heat capacity ( SI unti: $\cfrac{J}{kg\cdot K}$ )
    • $Q\quad$ Heat energy transferred ( SI unit: Joules (J) )
    • $m\quad$ Mass of the substance ( SI unit: kilogram (kg) )
    • $\Delta T\quad$ Temperature change ( unit: kelvin (K) or degrees Celsius ($^{\circ}C$) )

    Specific Heat Unit

    • SI Unit: In the International System is Joule per kilogram-Kelvin: $$c=1\;\cfrac{J}{kg\cdot K}$$

    Specific Heat Properties

    • Calorimeter:
      A calorimeter is a device used to measure the specific heat of a substance. It works by isolating a system and analyzing the heat exchange between substances to determine how much heat is required to change their temperature.
    • Intensive Property:
      Specific heat is an intensive physical quantity, meaning it does not depend on the amount of substance present. Instead, it is a characteristic property of the material itself.
    • Intrinsic Property of Materials:
      Specific heat is a fundamental property of materials and describes the amount of heat required to increase or decrease the temperature of a unit mass of a substance by one Kelvin. This means that different materials will require different amounts of heat to undergo the same temperature change.
