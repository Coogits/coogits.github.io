    Inertial Mass

    Inertial Mass (Mass) is the property of an object that quantifies its resistance to changes in motion when subjected to a force. It is defined through Newton's Second Law of Motion.
    In classical mechanics, inertial mass is considered equivalent to gravitational mass (the property that determines gravitational attraction), as confirmed by Einstein's Equivalence Principle in General Relativity.

    Formula

    • Newton's Second Law of Motion Definition: $$m=\cfrac{F}{a}$$

    Symbols

    • $m\quad$ Inertial Mass
    • $F\quad$ is the total force applied to the object,
    • $a\quad$ is the resulting Acceleration

    Characteristics:

    • It determines how much an object resists acceleration when a force is applied.
    • It is independent of location (e.g., it remains the same on Earth or in space).
    • It is measured in kilograms (kg) in the International System of Units (SI).
    • It is experimentally equivalent to gravitational mass (according to the equivalence principle in general relativity).

    Key Properties:

    • Frame Independence: Inertial mass is the same in all inertial reference frames.
    • Experimental Determination: It is measured by applying a known force and observing the resulting acceleration.
