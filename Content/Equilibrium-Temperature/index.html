    Equilibrium Temperature

    Equilibrium Temperature is the temperature at which two or more bodies in an isolated system reach the same temperature, so that there is no net exchange of heat between them. In other words, when the system is isolated, energy conservation requires that the heat lost by one part of the system equals the heat gained by another.

    Formulas

    • Equilibrium Temperature Between Two or More Bodies: This formula represents the weighted average of the initial temperatures, where the weights are the products of mass and specific heat capacity for each body: $$T_e=\cfrac{m_1c_1T_1+m_2c_2T_2+\ldots}{m_1c_1+m_2c_2+\ldots}$$
    • Equilibrium Temperature with a Phase Change: This equation accounts for a phase change (such as melting) in one of the substances. It includes the latent heat ($L_f$) and considers that the specific heat capacity may change after the phase transition (from $c_2$ to $c_2'$): $$T_e=\cfrac{T_{fus}m_2(c_2-c_2')-m_1c_1T_1-m_2c_2T_2+m_2L_f}{m_1c_1+m_2c_2'}$$

    Symbols

    • $T_e\quad$ Equilibrium Temperature (in Kelvin)
    • $m_1,m_2\quad$ Mass of the objects (in chilogrammi)
    • $T_1,T_2\quad$ Temperature of the objects (in Kelvin)
    • $c_1,c_2\quad$ Specific Heat of the objects
    • $T_{fus}\quad$ Temperature during the Phase Change (e.g., the melting point in Kelvin)
    • $c_2'\quad$ Specific Heat of the substance after the Phase Change ( unit: $\cfrac{J}{kg\cdot K}$ )
    • $L_f\quad$ Latent Heat of fusion (in J/kg)
