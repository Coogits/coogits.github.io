    Fluid

    Fluid is a substance in a liquid or gaseous state that can flow and deform continuously under the action of a shear stress. Fluids do not have a fixed shape and are defined by their ability to flow.

    Three States of Fluids:

    • Liquid: A fluid with a defined volume that is not easily compressible. The density is greater than that of gases but less than solids.
    • Gas: A fluid with no defined volume and can expand to fill any container. Gases are compressible, and their density is less than that of liquids but higher than that of plasma.
    • Plasma: A high-temperature state of matter where some electrons are stripped from atoms, creating free electrical charges and ions.

    Ideal vs. Real Fluids:

    • Ideal Fluid: A hypothetical fluid that is incompressible (density remains constant) and has negligible viscosity (no internal friction between layers).
    • Real Fluid: A fluid that is compressible and has viscosity, meaning it experiences internal friction and resistance to flow.

    Properties:

    • A fluid exerts pressure on both immersed objects and the walls of its container.
    • Incompressible Fluid: Typically, liquids that cannot be easily compressed, meaning their volume remains almost constant under pressure.
    • Compressible Fluid: Gases and fluids where the volume can change under pressure.
    • From a static point of view, ideal and real fluids behave similarly, but dynamic behavior requires distinctions (e.g., viscosity and compressibility).
    • Steady Flow: When the properties of a fluid, such as velocity, do not change over time at any given point, though they may vary spatially across different points in the fluid.
