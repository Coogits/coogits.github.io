    Phase Diagram

    A phase diagram is a graphical representation that shows the conditions of temperature, pressure, and sometimes composition under which distinct phases (such as solid, liquid, and gas) of a substance or mixture exist in equilibrium.


    ### Key Points

    Axes and Variables

    Typically, the diagram has temperature on one axis (often the horizontal axis) and pressure on the other (often the vertical axis).
    In systems with multiple components, additional axes or diagrams (e.g., ternary phase diagrams) are used to represent composition.

    Phase Regions

    The diagram is divided into regions, each corresponding to a different phase of the substance:

    • Solid Region
    • Liquid Region
    • Gas Region
      In some cases, additional regions may represent other phases, such as plasma or various crystalline forms.

    Phase Boundaries

    The lines or curves that separate these regions are known as phase boundaries. They indicate the conditions at which two phases coexist in equilibrium. For example:

    • The melting curve separates the solid and liquid regions.
    • The vaporization (or boiling) curve separates the liquid and gas regions.

    Special Points:

    • Triple Point: The unique set of conditions at which three phases (solid, liquid, and gas) coexist in equilibrium.
    • Critical Point: The end point of the vaporization curve, beyond which the liquid and gas phases become indistinguishable.
