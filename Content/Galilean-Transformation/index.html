    Galilean Transformation

    A Galilean transformation is a set of equations that relates the coordinates and time of an event between two inertial reference frames moving at a constant velocity relative to each other. It is used in classical mechanics and assumes that time is absolute


    ### Formulas
    • Standard Galilean Transformation (1D case): Describes how coordinates transform between two reference frames S and S', moving with a relative velocity v along the x-axis: $$x'=x-vt,\quad y'=y,\quad z'=z,\quad t'=t$$
    • General Vector Form: Expresses the transformation in a more general form, applying to motion in any direction: $$\begin{align}\vec r\;'=\vec r-\vec vt \\ t\;'=t\end{align}$$
    • Matrix Representation: Represents the transformation in Parametric vector form, useful for systematic calculations and multidimensional analysis: $$\begin{bmatrix}x' \\ y' \\ z' \\ t' \end{bmatrix}=\begin{bmatrix}x \\ y \\ z \\ t \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix}v_xt \\ v_yt \\ v_zt\\0 \end{bmatrix}$$

    Symbols

    • $x,y,z\quad$ Spatial coordinates in the original reference frame
    • $x',y',z'\quad$ Spatial coordinates in the moving reference frame
    • $t\quad$ Time in both frames (since Galilean transformations assume absolute time)
    • $\vec r\quad$ Position vector in the original frame
    • $\vec r\;'\quad$ Position vector in the moving frame
    • $v\quad$ Relative velocity of frame S' with respect to S along the x axis
    • $\vec v\quad$ Relative velocity of frame S' with respect to S in vector form
    • $v_x,v_y,v_z\quad$ Components of $\vec v$ along the coordinate axes

    Demonstration

    To Demonstrate the Galilean Transformation of a System $S'$ moving respect a system S:

    1. The position $\vec r$ of an object in the stationary system S is the sum of its position $\vec{r'}$ in the moving system S' and the displacement $\vec{r'}$ of S' relative to S: $$\vec{r}=\vec{r'}+\vec{r_0'}$$
    2. Expressing the displacement $\vec{r_0'}$ with Uniform Linear Motion: $$\vec{r}=\vec{r'}+\vec{v}t$$
    3. Apply the Properties of Equality to isolate $\vec{r'}$ and obtain the Galilean Transformation: $$\vec{r'}=\vec{r}-\vec{v}t$$

    Assumptions and Limitations

    Assumptions:

    • Absolute time: Time is the same in all reference frames.
    • Linear velocity addition: Velocities simply add up.

    Limitations:

    • Does not apply to objects moving at speeds close to the speed of light.
    • Replaced by Lorentz transformations in special relativity, where time is relative and space-time is unified.
