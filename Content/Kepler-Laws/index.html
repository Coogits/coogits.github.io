    Kepler's Laws

    Kepler's Laws of Planetary Motion describe the motion of planets around the Sun. They consist of three laws:

    1. Law of Orbits: All planets move in elliptical orbits with the Sun at one of the foci. This means that the path of a planet is not a perfect circle but an ellipse.

    2. Law of Areas: A line joining a planet and the Sun sweeps out equal areas during equal intervals of time. This implies that a planet moves faster when it is closer to the Sun and slower when it is farther away, in such a way that the area swept by the line joining the planet and the Sun is always constant.

    3. Law of Periods: The square of the orbital period of a planet is directly proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis of its orbit. Mathematically, this is expressed as: $$\cfrac{T^2}{r^2}=\text{constant}$$

      • $T\quad$ is the orbital period
      • $r\quad$ is the average distance from the Sun
