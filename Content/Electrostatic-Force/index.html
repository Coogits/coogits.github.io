    Electrostatic Force

    The Electrostatic Force (or Coulomb Force) is the force exerted between two charged particles due to their electric charges. It is described by Coulomb's Law, which states that the magnitude of the force between two point charges is directly proportional to the product of their charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

    Symbols

    • $F\quad$ Magnitude of the electrostatic force (unit: Newton (N))
    • $k=\cfrac{k_e}{\epsilon_r}\quad$ Electrostatic constant for a dielectric medium; in vacuum, $k=k_e$
    • $k_e\quad$ Coulomb's Constant in a vacuum ( $k_e=\cfrac{1}{4\pi \epsilon_0}\simeq8.99\times10^9\;\cfrac{N\cdot{m^2}}{C^2}$)
    • $\epsilon_0\quad$ vacuum permittivity ($\epsilon_0\simeq8,854\cdot10^{-12}\;\frac{C^2}{N\cdot m^2}$)
    • $\epsilon_r\quad$ relative permittivity in a dielectric ($\epsilon_r=\cfrac{F_0}{F_m}$, dimensionless )
    • $\lvert q_1\rvert\lvert q_2\rvert\quad$ Magnitudes of the two Electric Charge ( unit: Coulomb (C) )
    • $r\quad$ Distance between the charges ( unit: meters (m) )
    • $\vec F\quad$ Electrostatic Force vector form ( unit: Newton (N) )
    • $\vec{s_2}-\vec{s_1}\quad$ Position vector difference, displacement between charges.
    • $\hat r\quad$ is the Unit vector pointing from charge $q_1$ to the charge $q_2$, indicating the force direction

    Formulas

    • Coulomb's Law (scalar form): $$F=k\cfrac{\lvert q_1\rvert\lvert q_2\rvert}{r^2}$$
    • Coulomb's Law (vector form): $$\vec{F}=k\cfrac{q_1q_2}{r^2}\hat{r}=kq_1q_2\cfrac{\vec{s_2}-\vec{s_1}}{\rvert\vec{s_2}-\vec{s_1}\lvert^3}$$

    Unit

    • SI Unit: Since the Coulomb force is a type of force, its SI unit is the Newton (N): $$F=\left(\cfrac{N\cdot{m^2}}{C^2}\right)\cdot\left(\cfrac{C^2}{m^2}\right)=N$$

    Properties

    • Conservative Force:
      The Coulomb force is a conservative force, meaning that the work done by this force in moving a charge between two points is independent of the path taken and depends only on the initial and final positions.

    • Superposition Principle:
      When multiple charges are present, the total force on a given charge is the vector sum of the individual forces exerted by each charge. Mathematically: $$\vec F_{tot}=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}\vec{F_i}$$

    • Inverse-Square Law Interpretation:
      Coulombs law follows an inverse-square dependence on the distance between charges. This means that doubling the distance between two charges reduces the force to one-fourth of its original value: $$F\propto r^{-2}$$
