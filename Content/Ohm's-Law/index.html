    Ohm's Law

    Ohm's Law is a fundamental principle in electrical circuits that states that the voltage $V$ across a conductor is directly proportional to the current (III) flowing through it, provided the resistance $R$ remains constant.


    Symbols

    • $\Delta{V}\quad$ is the Voltage (in volts, $V$),
    • $I\quad$ is the Electric Current (in amperes, A),
    • $R\quad$ is the Electrical Resistance (in ohms, $\Omega$).

    Formulas

    • Ohm's Law:
      Is defined as $$\Delta{V}=IR$$
    • Rearrangement:
      If resistance is known, the equation can be rearranged as: $$I=\cfrac{\Delta{V}}{R}$$ or $$R=\cfrac{\Delta{V}}{I}$$

    Properties

    • Ohmâ€™s Law applies to linear resistors, where the resistance remains constant regardless of the voltage or current.
    • Ohm's Law is widely used in circuit analysis to determine voltage, current, and resistance in electrical components.
    • It does not apply to non-ohmic materials, such as semiconductors or components like diodes, which do not have a constant resistance.

    Ohm's Law Triangle

    Ohm's Law Triangle is a way to help memorize the relationships. We can imagine the line under the Voltage $V$ as a fraction like:

    • $=\begin{cases}V=IR\\I=\cfrac{V}{R}\\R=\cfrac{V}{I}\end{cases}$
