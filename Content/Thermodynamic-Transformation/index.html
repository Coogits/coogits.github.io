    Thermodynamic Transformation

    A thermodynamic transformation (or thermodynamic process) is any change that a thermodynamic system undergoes which results in a variation of its state variables (such as pressure, volume, temperature, and internal energy). Here are the key points:

    • Change of State:
      A thermodynamic transformation moves a system from one equilibrium state to another. During the transformation, properties like pressure, volume, and temperature can change.

    • Energy Interactions:
      During the process, energy can be exchanged with the surroundings in the form of heat transfer and work. The way these energy exchanges occur helps to classify the transformation.

    • Types of Processes:
      Thermodynamic transformations are often categorized based on the conditions under which they occur:

      • Isothermal Transformation: Temperature remains constant.
      • Isobaric Transformation: Pressure remains constant.
      • Isochoric Transformation Volume remains constant.
      • Adiabatic: No heat is exchanged with the surroundings.
      • Polytropic: The process follows a specific relation between pressure and volume.

    • Reversibility:
      Transformations can be reversible (idealized processes that can be exactly reversed by an infinitesimal change in conditions) or irreversible (real processes where returning to the original state requires changes that increase the entropy of the universe).

    • Process Path:
      In the context of equilibrium thermodynamics, although the initial and final states are equilibrium states, the process path may not always be quasi-static (i.e., it might involve non-equilibrium states during the transformation). For idealized analyses, processes are often assumed to be quasi-static to simplify calculations.
