    Electric Potential

    The electric potential $V$ at a point in an electric field is the electric potential energy per unit charge at that point. It represents the work required to move a unit positive charge from a reference point (typically infinity) to that location, without acceleration.

    In the image:
    The electric potential around two oppositely charged conducting spheres, where purple indicates the highest potential, yellow represents zero potential, and cyan denotes the lowest potential.

    Symbols

    • $V\quad$ Electric Potential (unit: Volt (V))
    • $\Delta V\quad$ Electric Potential Difference (unit: Volt (V))
    • $V(r)\quad$ Electric Potential at distance $r$ (unit: Volt (V))
    • $Q\quad$ source Charge (unit: Coulombs (C))
    • $q\quad$ test Charge (unit: Coulombs (C))
    • $U\quad$ Electric Potential Energy (unit: Joule (J))
    • $\epsilon_0\quad$ vacuum permittivity, a fundamental constant ($\epsilon_0\simeq8,854\cdot10^{-12}\;\frac{C^2}{N\cdot m^2}$)

    Formulas

    • Definition:
      Electric potential is defined as the potential energy per unit charge: $$V=\cfrac{U}{q}$$
    • Electric Potential Due to a Point Charge:
      The electric potential at a distance $r$ from a charge $Q$ in vacuum: $$V(r)=\cfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\cfrac{Q}{r}$$

    Voltage

    Voltage (potential difference) is the difference in electric potential between two points.
    It represents the energy per unit charge required to move a charge between two points in an electric field.

    • Electric Potential Difference (Voltage):
      The difference in potential between two points A and B in an electric field, in this case A with respect to B: $$\Delta{V}=\cfrac{\Delta{U}}{q}=\cfrac{-W}{q}=V_A-V_B$$ For a charge $Q$ at distances $r_A$ and $r_B$, in this case A with respect to B: $$\Delta V=\cfrac{Q}{4\pi\epsilon_0}\left(\cfrac{1}{r_A}-\cfrac{1}{r_B}\right)$$

    Units

    • SI Units: $$V=\left(\cfrac{J}{C}\right)=\color{red}V$$
