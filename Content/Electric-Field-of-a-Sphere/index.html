    Electric Field of a Sphere

    The electric field of a sphere depends on whether the sphere is conducting or non-conducting (charged insulating sphere) and follows different rules based on Gauss's Law.

    Symbols

    • $E(r)\quad$ Electric Field at a distance $r$ from the center
    • $Q\quad$ Total Electric Charge on the sphere
    • $\epsilon\quad$ Absolute Permittivity in the medium ($\epsilon=\epsilon_0\epsilon_r$)
    • $\rho\quad$ Volume Charge Density (for a uniformly charged sphere)
    • $R\quad$ Radius of the sphere

    Formulas

    • Outside the sphere ($r>R$) Same for both hollow and full spheres:
      The sphere behaves like a point charge: $$E(r)=\cfrac{1}{4\pi\epsilon}\cfrac{Q}{r^2}$$
    • Inside the hollow (conducting) sphere ($r<R$):
      The electric field inside a conducting sphere is always zero: $$E(r)=0$$
    • Inside a uniformly charged full sphere ($r<R$):
      The electric field increases linearly with $r$: $$E(r)=\cfrac{\rho r}{3\epsilon}$$

    Demonstration

    • Electric Field Inside a Hollow Sphere ($r<R$):
      Applying Gauss's Theorem: $$\begin{align} \Phi_E &= \cfrac{Q_{int}}{\epsilon} \quad&\impliedby\quad &\text{Gauss's theorem} \\ \Phi_E &= \cfrac{0}{\epsilon} = 0 \quad&\impliedby\quad &\text{no charge is present inside the sphere} \end{align}$$
    • Electric Field Inside a Solid Sphere ($r<R$):
      Applying Charge Density to Gauss's Theorem: $$\begin{align} Q_{int} &= \rho V = \rho \frac{4}{3} \pi r^3 \quad&\impliedby\quad &\rho = \cfrac{Q}{V} \text{ (volume charge density)} \\ \Phi_E &= \cfrac{Q_{int}}{\epsilon} = \cfrac{4\pi \rho r^3}{3\epsilon} \quad&\impliedby\quad &\text{Gauss's theorem} \\ E \cdot 4\pi r^2 &= \cfrac{4\pi \rho r^3}{3\epsilon} \quad&\impliedby\quad &\text{flux through a spherical surface} \\ E &= \cfrac{\rho r}{3\epsilon} \quad&\impliedby\quad &\text{simplifying terms} \end{align}$$
