    Euclidean Geometry

    Euclidean Geometry is a branch of mathematics that studies the properties and relationships of points, lines, angles, surfaces, and solids in a flat, two-dimensional or three-dimensional space, based on the axioms and postulates formulated by the ancient Greek mathematician Euclid in his work Elements.

    Key Concepts

    • Pythagorean Theorem
    • Line (Euclidean Geometry)
    • Angle (Euclidean Geometry)

    Sub-Fields

    Plane Geometry

    Plane geometry is a branch of Euclidean geometry that focuses on the study of shapes, figures, and their properties in a flat, two-dimensional surface known as a plane.

    • Quadrilateral
    • Triangle
    • Circle

    Solid Geometry

    Solid geometry is the branch of geometry that deals with the study of three-dimensional shapes (also called solids) and their properties, such as volume, surface area, edges, vertices, and faces.

    Axiomatic Framework

    Euclidean geometry is built on five fundamental postulates (Euclid's postulates), which describe basic geometric relationships:

    • A straight line can be drawn connecting any two points.
    • A finite straight line can be extended indefinitely in a straight line.
    • A circle can be drawn with any center and any radius.
    • All right angles are congruent.
    • (Parallel Postulate) If a straight line intersects two straight lines and forms interior angles on the same side that are less than two right angles, then the two straight lines, if extended indefinitely, meet on that side.

    Flat Space

    Euclidean geometry assumes a flat, two-dimensional plane (or three-dimensional space) where the rules of distance, angles, and shapes follow the familiar properties taught in elementary geometry.

    Applications

    Euclidean geometry underpins much of classical mathematics and physics, forming the basis for fields like architecture, engineering, and computer graphics.

    Limitations

    It applies only in flat spaces. For curved surfaces or spaces, non-Euclidean geometries (e.g., spherical or hyperbolic geometry) are required.
