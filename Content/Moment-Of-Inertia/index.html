    Moment of Inertia

    The moment of inertia (often denoted as III) of a rigid body is a measure of the body's resistance to changes in its rotational motion about a specific axis. It depends on the mass distribution of the object relative to the axis of rotation: the further the mass is from the axis, the greater the moment of inertia.

    Symbols

    • $I\quad$ Moment of Inertia
    • $m\quad$ Inertial Mass (in kilogram (kg))
    • $r\quad$ is the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation to the mass.

    Formulas

    • Moment of Inertia:
      for a point mass, the moment of inertia about an axis is given by: $$I=mr^2$$
    • For an extended rigid body:
      the moment of inertia is the sum (or Integral Calculus) of the moments of inertia of all the individual mass elements in the body. In the continuous case, the formula becomes: $$I=\displaystyle\int r^2dm$$
    • Superposition Principle:
      for a system of points the Superposition Principle can be applied to the moment of inertia: $$I=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n m_ir_i^2$$

    Unit

    • The SI symbols is the kilogram-meter squared $(kg\cdot m^2)$: $$I=kg\cdot m^2$$

    Key Points:

    1. The moment of inertia depends on the mass distribution relative to the axis of rotation.
    2. For rigid bodies with uniform density, the moment of inertia can often be calculated using known formulas for common shapes (e.g., spheres, cylinders).
    3. The moment of inertia plays a crucial role in rotational dynamics, analogous to mass in linear motion. For example, the rotational analog of Newton's second law is: $$\vec\tau=I\vec\alpha$$
      • $\vec\tau\quad$ Torque (Physics)
      • $\vec\alpha\quad$ Angular Acceleration
