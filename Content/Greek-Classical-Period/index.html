    Greek Classical Period

    • Start: c. 500 BCE
    • End: 323 BCE

    Timeline

    Key Features

    Rise of Athenian Democracy

    • The Classical Period saw the development of Athenian democracy under leaders like Pericles...
    • Direct democracy allowed citizens (free adult males) to participate in decision-making...
    • Reforms by Solon, Cleisthenes, and Pericles helped shape the democratic system, with the Assembly and Council of 500 playing key roles.

    Political and Military Conflicts

    • The Persian Wars (499-449 BCE) were pivotal, with Greek city-states, especially Athens and Sparta, uniting to resist Persian expansion...
    • The victory at Marathon (490 BCE) and the stand at Thermopylae (480 BCE) bolstered Greek pride...
    • The Peloponnesian War (431-404 BCE) between Athens and Sparta weakened the Greek world, leading to the eventual rise of Macedonia.

    Art and Architecture

    • Greek architecture reached new heights with the construction of temples like the Parthenon on the Acropolis in Athens...
    • Doric, Ionic, and Corinthian orders defined Greek temples and public buildings...
    • Classical sculpture became more realistic and idealized, with figures like Phidias, Myron, and Polykleitos creating famous works like the Discobolus (Discus Thrower) and Doryphoros (Spear Bearer).

    Philosophical Developments

    • Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle revolutionized Western philosophy, focusing on ethics, politics, and the nature of knowledge...
    • Socrates questioned traditional values and ideas through dialectic methods, encouraging critical thinking...
    • Plato, his student, founded the Academy and proposed the theory of forms...
    • Aristotle, Plato's student, made contributions to logic, ethics, metaphysics, and natural science, founding the Lyceum.

    Literature and Drama

    • Tragedy and comedy flourished in Athens during the Classical Period, with playwrights like Aeschylus, Sophocles, and Euripides shaping Greek drama...
    • Sophocles wrote Oedipus Rex and Antigone, exploring themes of fate and morality...
    • Aristophanes became the leading comic playwright, satirizing Athenian society in works like Lysistrata and The Clouds...
    • Herodotus and Thucydides revolutionized historical writing, focusing on empirical methods and analysis.

    Science and Mathematics

    • Greek thinkers made significant contributions to mathematics, astronomy, and medicine...
    • Euclid formalized geometry in his work Elements, shaping mathematics for centuries...
    • Pythagoras and Euclid developed foundational concepts in geometry, with Euclidâ€™s Elements becoming a key work in mathematics...
    • Hipparchus made early advancements in astronomy, including the development of the star catalog and the discovery of the precession of the equinoxes...
    • Hippocrates, often called the father of medicine, introduced rational approaches to diagnosing and treating illness.

    Cultural and Intellectual Life

    • Athens became the cultural and intellectual center of the Greek world, with the Acropolis and Agora as hubs for artistic, philosophical, and political activity...
    • The Delian League, initially a military alliance, became a means for Athens to exert economic and cultural dominance over the Greek world...
    • The Olympic Games continued to be an important cultural and religious event, held every four years in honor of Zeus.

    Decline and Macedonian Rise

    • The Peloponnesian War (431-404 BCE) left Athens weakened and vulnerable...
    • The rise of Macedonia under King Philip II and his son Alexander the Great marked the end of the Classical Greek period...
    • Alexander's conquests led to the spread of Greek culture throughout the Mediterranean and Asia, initiating the Hellenistic Period.

    Scientific and Intellectual Achievements

    • Aristotle made lasting contributions to philosophy, logic, and natural sciences, laying the foundation for much of medieval and modern thought...
    • Hippocrates revolutionized medicine with an empirical, systematic approach, advocating for ethical medical practice, encapsulated in the Hippocratic Oath.
