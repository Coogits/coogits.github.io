    Physical System

    A physical system is a well-defined part of the universe or a specific portion of space-time selected for analysis. It is characterized by a collection of matter and energy, and its interactions can be described by the laws of physics. Systems are usually studied in isolation, meaning that interactions with external environments (the surroundings) are either neglected or modeled explicitly.

    Concepts

    • Material Point:
      A simplified model of a physical system where the object is treated as a point with mass but no size or shape. It is often used in basic mechanics to simplify the analysis of motion.
    • Rigid Body:
      A physical system where the distances between particles (components of the body) remain constant, regardless of external forces or internal deformations. It is used to study the motion of objects that do not change shape or size under stress.
    • Center of Mass:
      The weighted average position of all the mass in a system, treated as a single point for the purposes of analyzing the system's motion. It is a crucial concept in both dynamics and statics when studying the behavior of a system.

      definition
    • Degree of Freedom (DOF):
      Refers to the number of independent parameters or coordinates required to fully describe the state or configuration of the system.

    Characteristics

    Isolated System

    These are systems that do not exchange energy or matter with their surroundings. An isolated system is a conceptual idealization used to simplify analyses, especially when studying conservation laws, such as the conservation of energy or momentum.

    Closed Systems

    These systems can exchange energy with the environment but not matter. They are often considered when studying thermodynamics or other phenomena where energy is transferred but mass remains constant.

    Open Systems

    These systems can exchange both energy and matter with their environment. Examples include living organisms or engines where both heat and material can be exchanged with the surroundings.

    Inertial Frames of Reference

    A physical system is often analyzed from an inertial reference frame, where no external forces are acting on the observer. In an inertial frame, objects either remain at rest or continue to move with constant velocity unless acted upon by a force.

    Non-inertial Frames of Reference

    If the system is observed from a non-inertial reference frame (such as a rotating or accelerating reference frame), additional fictitious forces (e.g., centrifugal force) must be introduced to account for the perceived motion of the system.
