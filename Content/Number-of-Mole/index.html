    Number of Mole

    The number of moles is a measure of the amount of substance in a system. It is defined as the ratio of the total number of elementary entities (such as atoms, molecules, ions, etc.) present in the substance to Avogadro's number.

    Symbols

    • $n\quad$ amount of substance (unit: mole (mol))
    • $N\quad$ total number of particles
    • $N_A\quad$ Avogadro Constant ($N_A=6.02214076\times10^{23} \frac{\text{ entities}}{mol}$)
    • $u\quad$ atomic mass unit
    • $m\quad$ Mass of the substance (in grams, g)
    • $M\quad$ Molar Mass (unit: $\cfrac{g}{mol}$)
    • $^{12}C\quad$ Carbon-12

    Formulas

    • One Mole:
      One mole is defined as the number of atoms in 12 grams of carbon-12 ($^{12}C$): $$n=\,^{12}C\cdot12\text{ g}$$ One mole of any substance contains exactly $6.02214076\cdot10^{23}$ elementary entities. This number is known as Avogadro's number: $$n=N_A\text{ entities}$$
    • Number of Mole Relations:
      The number of moles $n$ is related to the total number of particles $N$, the mass $m$, and the molar mass $M$ as follows: $$n=\cfrac{N}{N_A}=\cfrac{m}{M}=\cfrac{Nu}{M}$$

    Examples

    Carbon

    Find the mole of 24 grams of Carbon:

    • $M=12\, \text{g}$ (the Molar Mass for each element can be found in the Periodic Table)
    • $n=\cfrac{24\,\text{g}}{12\,\text{g}}=2\,\text{mol}$

    Water

    find the mole of 24 grams of water

    • Find the Molar Mass
      • Hydrogen (H): 1.01 g/mol × 2 = 2.02 g/mol
      • Oxygen (O): 16.00 g/mol
        Molar mass of H₂O = 2.02 + 16.00 = 18.02 g/mol
    • $n=\cfrac{24}{18.02}\approx1.333$
