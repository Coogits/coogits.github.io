    Pascal's Principle

    Pascal's Principle states that when a change in pressure is applied to an enclosed fluid, the pressure change is transmitted equally and undiminished in all directions throughout the fluid. This means that any pressure applied to a confined fluid will result in an equal increase in pressure at every point within the fluid.

    In simpler terms: If you apply a force to a fluid in a closed container, the resulting pressure is felt everywhere within the fluid, and it acts in all directions.

    Formulas

    • Pascal's Principle: Describes the relationship between the force and area in an enclosed fluid: $$\cfrac{F_1}{A_1}=\cfrac{F_2}{A_2}$$

    Symbols

    • $F_1, F_2\quad$ are the forces applied on areas $A_1$ and $A_2$ respectively.
    • $A_1, A_2\quad$ are the areas where the forces are applied.

    Demonstration

    1. Pressure: For an enclosed fluid, if a force $F_1$ is applied to an area $A_1$, the pressure $p_1$ created is: $$p_1=\cfrac{F_1}{A_1}$$
    2. According to Pascal's Principle, if this pressure is transmitted undiminished to another area $A_2$, the force $F_2$ exerted on $A_2$ is: $$F_2=P_1A_2$$
    3. Since the pressure $p_1$ is the same at all points, we can conclude: $$\cfrac{F_1}{A_1}=\cfrac{F_2}{A_2}$$
