    Metal, Non-Metal, Metalloid

    Metals, non-metals, and metalloids are classifications of elements based on their physical and chemical properties.
    They differ in their conductivity, appearance, malleability, and chemical behavior.

    Metals non-Metals and Metalloids

    A list of Metals (Blue), non-Metals (red), Metalloids (green)

    Metal

    Metals are typically shiny, good conductors of heat and electricity, malleable, and ductile, and they tend to lose electrons in reactions.
    A metal is an element that typically:

    • Loses electrons to form positive ions (cations)
    • Is lustrous (shiny)
    • Is a good conductor of heat and electricity
    • Is malleable and ductile
    • Usually solid at room temperature (except mercury, Hg)

    Found mostly on the left and center of the periodic table (Groups 1â€“12, s- and d-blocks)

    Examples:

    • Sodium (Na), Iron (Fe), Copper (Cu), Aluminum (Al)

    Non-Metal

    Non-metals are generally dull, poor conductors, brittle in solid form, and tend to gain or share electrons.
    A non-metal is an element that typically:

    • Gains or shares electrons to form negative ions (anions) or covalent bonds
    • Is dull (non-lustrous)
    • Is a poor conductor (insulator)
    • Is brittle in solid form
    • Can be solid, liquid, or gas at room temperature

    Found on the right side of the periodic table (p-block), plus Hydrogen

    Examples:

    • Oxygen (O), Chlorine (Cl), Sulfur (S), Carbon (C)

    Metalloid

    Metalloids have properties intermediate between metals and non-metals; they can appear shiny or dull and often act as semiconductors.

    • Can be semiconductors (conduct electricity under certain conditions)
    • May be shiny like metals but brittle like non-metals
    • Show variable chemical behavior (can gain, lose, or share electrons depending on the reaction)

    Found along the stair-step line (zigzag) between metals and non-metals on the periodic table

    Examples:

    • Silicon (Si), Boron (B), Arsenic (As), Antimony (Sb), Tellurium (Te)
