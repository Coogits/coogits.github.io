    Molar Specific Heat Capacity

    Molar specific heat capacity, often simply called molar specific heat, is defined as the amount of heat energy required to raise the temperature of one mole of a substance by one kelvin (or one degree Celsius). It is an intrinsic property of a material that indicates its ability to store heat, independent of the amount of substance present.

    Formulas

    • General Molar Specific Heat: The total heat exchanged when the temperature of moles of a substance changes by $\Delta T$ is given by: $$Q=nC_m\Delta{T}$$
    • Molar Specific Heat Capacity At Constant Pressure (isobar): describes the heat required to raise the temperature of one mole of a substance at constant pressure: $$c_p=\cfrac{1}{n}\left(\cfrac{dQ}{dT}\right)_p$$
    • Molar Specific Heat Capacity At Constant Volume (Isochore): describes the heat required to raise the temperature of one mole of a substance at constant volume: $$c_v=\cfrac{1}{n}\left(\cfrac{dQ}{dT}\right)_v$$

    Symbols

    • $Q\quad$ is the Heat exchanged, ( unit: joule (J) )
    • $n\quad$ number of moles ( unit: mole (mol) ),
    • $C_m\quad$ molar specific heat capacity ( SI unti: $\cfrac{J}{kg\cdot K}$ ),
    • $\Delta T\quad$ is the Temperature change ( unit: kelvin (K) or degrees Celsius ($^{\circ}C$) ).
    • $c_p\quad$ Molar specific heat Capacity at constant pressure
    • $c_v\quad$ Molar specific heat Capacity at constant volume.
