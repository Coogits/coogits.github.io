    Babylonian

    • Start: c. 2.000 BCE
    • End: 539 BCE

    Timeline

    Periods

    Old Babylonian Period

    (c. 1894–1595 BCE)

    • Babylon starts as a small city-state under the Amorite dynasty.
    • Hammurabi (r. 1792–1750 BCE) unifies much of Mesopotamia.
      • Creates the Code of Hammurabi, one of the earliest legal codes.
    • Babylon becomes the dominant power in the region.
    • Ends when the Hittites sack Babylon (1595 BCE), leading to foreign rule.

    Middle Babylonian Period

    (c. 1595–1155 BCE)

    • Kassites, a foreign dynasty, rule Babylon for over 400 years.
    • Babylon remains a major cultural and religious center.
    • Trade and diplomacy flourish (relations with Egypt and Assyria).
    • Eventually, Babylon is weakened by Assyrian and Elamite invasions.
    • Ends when the Elamites sack Babylon in 1155 BCE.

    Neo-Babylonian Empire

    (c. 626–539 BCE)

    • Babylon revives as an independent empire after throwing off Assyrian rule.
    • Major rulers:
      • Nabopolassar (r. 626–605 BCE) – Helps destroy the Assyrian Empire (612 BCE).
      • Nebuchadnezzar II (r. 605–562 BCE) – Expands the empire, destroys Jerusalem (586 BCE), and builds the Hanging Gardens.
      • Nabonidus (r. 556–539 BCE) – His religious policies cause unrest.
    • Ends when Cyrus the Great of Persia conquers Babylon (539 BCE).

    Late Babylonian Period

    (539–c. 141 BCE)

    • Babylon is ruled by Persians (539–331 BCE) under the Achaemenid Empire.
    • Briefly ruled by Alexander the Great (331 BCE) and later by the Seleucid Empire.
    • Babylon declines as a major city, with the rise of new Hellenistic centers.
    • Eventually, it is abandoned as a political power by the Parthian period (c. 141 BCE).

    Key Features of Babylonia

    Rise and Expansion

    • Babylonia emerged as a significant power in the ancient Near East, centered around the city of Babylon.
    • It rose to prominence under King Hammurabi (reigned c. 1792–1750 BCE), who united much of Mesopotamia and established the Babylonian Empire.

    Code of Hammurabi

    • King Hammurabi is best known for creating one of the earliest and most complete written legal codes, the Code of Hammurabi.
    • This law code, inscribed on a stone pillar, covered various aspects of daily life, including trade, property, marriage, and criminal justice, and is famous for its principle of "an eye for an eye."

    Cultural Achievements

    • Babylonian culture was a blend of Sumerian traditions and innovations, but with significant developments of its own.
    • They made advances in mathematics, astronomy, and astrology, with the development of a sexagesimal (base-60) number system, which is still used to measure time today.
    • The Babylonians were among the first to develop astronomical tables and make predictions about celestial movements.

    Architecture and the Hanging Gardens

    • The city of Babylon was known for its impressive architecture, including the Ishtar Gate, adorned with blue glazed bricks and depictions of animals like lions.
    • The Hanging Gardens of Babylon, one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World (though its existence is debated), were said to be a grand terraced garden built by King Nebuchadnezzar II for his wife.

    Religion and Mythology

    • Babylonian religion was polytheistic, with gods such as Marduk (the chief god of Babylon) playing an essential role in their pantheon.
    • The Babylonians practiced extensive rituals, including offerings, prayers, and festivals dedicated to their gods, especially Marduk.
    • The Enuma Elish, a Babylonian creation myth, described the creation of the world and the rise of Marduk as the chief deity.

    Decline and Fall

    • After a period of prosperity, Babylon fell into decline due to internal strife and external invasions.
    • In 539 BCE, Cyrus the Great of Persia conquered Babylon, ending its role as a dominant power in the region.
