    Neolithic

    The Neolithic (or New Stone Age) was a major turning point in human history, marked by the shift from hunting and gathering to agriculture and permanent settlements. It lasted roughly from 8,000 BCE to 3,000 BCE, though the exact timeline varies by region.

    • Start: 8,000 BCE
    • End: 3,000 BCE

    Timeline

    Key Features

    • Agriculture & Domestication: People began farming crops (wheat, barley, rice) and domesticating animals (cows, sheep, goats, pigs) for food and labor.

    • Permanent Settlements: Villages and towns like Çatalhöyük (Turkey) and Jericho (Palestine) developed, leading to organized societies.

    • Advanced Tools & Pottery: Polished stone tools replaced rough Paleolithic tools, and pottery was used for cooking and storage.

    • Weaving & Textile Production: The development of woven fabrics led to better clothing and trade opportunities.

    • Social Structure & Specialization: Different jobs emerged, such as farmers, potters, and builders, leading to a more complex society.

    • Religious Practices & Megalithic Structures: Early religious sites, like Stonehenge, suggest spiritual beliefs and rituals.

    The Neolithic Revolution laid the foundation for civilization, eventually leading to the Bronze Age, where metal tools replaced stone tools, and the first cities and writing systems emerged.
