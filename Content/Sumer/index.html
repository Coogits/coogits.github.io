    Sumer

    Timeline

    Periods

    Early Sumerian Period

    (c. 4500–2900 BCE)

    Ubaid Culture

    (c. 4500–3100 BCE)

    • First known settlers in southern Mesopotamia.
    • Early irrigation, temple-building, and social stratification.
    Uruk Period

    (c. 4100–2900 BCE)

    • Rise of Uruk, the first major city.
    • Development of writing (cuneiform), cylinder seals, and large temples (ziggurats).
    • Expansion of trade networks across Mesopotamia and beyond.

    Early Dynastic Period

    (c. 2900–2334 BCE)

    Formation of City-States
    • Independent cities such as Ur, Uruk, Lagash, Kish, and Nippur emerge.
    • Each city-state ruled by a king (Lugal) and a temple complex (Ensi or priest-king).
    Warfare and Rivalries
    • Frequent conflicts between city-states for resources and dominance.
    • King Eannatum of Lagash (c. 2500 BCE) expands his rule, creating an early empire.
    Cultural and Technological Advances
    • Development of law codes, literature (Epic of Gilgamesh), and mathematics.
    • Invention of the wheel, bronze tools, and organized religion.

    Akkadian Conquest and Influence

    (c. 2334–2154 BCE)

    Rise of Sargon of Akkad

    (c. 2334 BCE)

    • Sargon the Great conquers Sumer, creating the Akkadian Empire.
    • Standardizes administration and spreads Akkadian language.
    Sumerian Rebellion and Decline of Akkad
    • Sumerian city-states resist Akkadian rule.
    • The Akkadian Empire collapses due to internal strife and Gutian invasions (c. 2154 BCE).

    Neo-Sumerian Period

    (Ur III Dynasty) (c. 2112–2004 BCE)

    Sumerian Revival under Ur-Nammu
    • Ur-Nammu (r. 2112–2095 BCE) reestablishes Sumerian rule.
    • Constructs Ziggurat of Ur and issues one of the first law codes.
    Flourishing of Art, Literature, and Administration
    • Standardization of weights, measures, and taxation.
    • Growth of trade with the Indus Valley and Persian Gulf.
    Collapse of the Ur III Dynasty (2004 BCE)
    • Invaded by Elamites and Amorites, leading to Sumer’s decline.

    Final Decline and Amorite Takeover

    (c. 2004–1750 BCE)

    Rise of Amorite Kings
    • Amorites take control of major cities like Babylon, Isin, and Larsa.
    • Sumerian language fades as Akkadian becomes dominant.
    Hammurabi of Babylon

    (r. 1792–1750 BCE)

    • Conquers the last Sumerian city-states, fully absorbing Sumer into the Babylonian Empire.
    • Marks the end of Sumer as an independent civilization.
