    Permittivity

    Permittivity ($\epsilon$) is a fundamental property of materials that quantifies their ability to permit the formation of an electric field within them. It describes how an electric field interacts with a medium, influencing the amount of electric flux generated per unit charge.

    Vacuum Permittivity($\epsilon_0$), also known as the electric constant, is a fundamental physical constant that characterizes the ability of free space to support an electric field. It plays a crucial role in Coulomb's Law, Gauss's Law, and Maxwell's equations, establishing the relationship between electric charge and the resulting electric field in a vacuum.

    Symbols

    • $F_0\quad$ Electrostatic Force in a vacuum
    • $F_m\quad$ Electrostatic Force in a dielectric medium

    Formulas

    • Vacuum Permittivity:
      The permittivity of free space, applicable when charges are in a vacuum: $$\epsilon_0\simeq8.854\,187\,818\times10^{-12}\;\cfrac{C^2}{N\cdot m^2}$$
    • Relative Permittivity (dielectric constant):
      When charges are in a dielectric medium, the relative permittivity describes how the medium affects the electrostatic force compared to a vacuum:: $$\epsilon_r=\cfrac{F_0}{F_m}$$
    • Absolute Permittivity:
      The permittivity of a dielectric medium is given by the product of vacuum permittivity and the relative permittivity of the material: $$\epsilon=\epsilon_0\epsilon_r$$
