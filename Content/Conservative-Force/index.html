    Conservative Force

    A conservative force is one where the work done over a closed path is zero, meaning the force depends only on the initial and final positions, not the path taken. This implies that energy is conserved, with no loss or gain over a round trip. Conservative forces are linked to potential energy, which depends on the object's position. The work done by such a force can be expressed as the change in potential energy, ensuring that energy is conserved in the system.

    1. Work is zero:
      The work done by a conservative force $\vec{F}$ over a closed path $\gamma$ is zero. This means that the line integral of the force over any closed loop results in zero: $$\displaystyle\oint_{\gamma}\vec{F}\cdot\vec{s}=0$$
    2. Path independence:
      The work done by a conservative force is independent of the path taken between two points. This means the line integral of the force from point $A$ to point $B$ yields the same result, whether the path is path 1 or path 2: $$\overbrace{\displaystyle\int_A^B\vec{F}\cdot\vec{s}}^{path1}=\overbrace{\displaystyle\int_A^B\vec{F}\cdot\vec{s}}^{path2}$$
    3. Potential energy exists:
      A conservative force can be associated with a potential energy function, where the work done by the force is related to the change in potential energy. The potential energy function $U$ allows us to define the work done as the negative change in potential energy: $$W=-\Delta U$$
