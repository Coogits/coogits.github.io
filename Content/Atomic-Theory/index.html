    Atomic Theory

    Atomic theory is a scientific framework that explains the composition, structure, and behavior of matter in terms of atoms—the smallest units of an element that retain its chemical properties.
    It describes how atoms interact, combine, and rearrange in chemical processes, forming the basis of chemistry and materials science.

    Atom Structure

    Atoms are not indivisible — they are composed of smaller subatomic particles.

    Proton (Atom)

    Positively charged particles located in the nucleus.

    Neutron (Atom)

    Neutral particles also found in the nucleus.

    Electron (Atom)

    Negatively charged particles that orbit the nucleus in energy levels.

    History

    1. Democritus

    • (~400 BCE) – The Origin of the Atom
    • Who: Greek philosopher
    • Contribution: First proposed that all matter is made of small, indivisible units called atomos ("uncuttable").
    • Significance: Although not based on experiments, his idea laid the philosophical groundwork for atomic theory.

    "Nothing exists except atoms and empty space; everything else is opinion." – Democritus

    2. John Dalton

    • (1766–1844) – First Scientific Atomic Theory
    • Who: English chemist and physicist
    • Contribution: Formulated the first modern atomic theory based on scientific experiments.
    • Major Points:
      • All elements are made of atoms.
      • Atoms of a given element are identical in mass and properties.
      • Atoms combine in whole-number ratios to form compounds.
      • Atoms are rearranged in reactions, not created or destroyed.

    Dalton’s model explained the law of conservation of mass and the law of definite proportions.

    3. J.J. Thomson

    • (1856–1940) – Discovery of the Electron
    • Who: British physicist
    • Contribution: Discovered the electron through cathode ray experiments (1897).
    • Model: Proposed the Plum Pudding Model, where electrons were embedded in a positive "soup" of charge.
    • Impact: Showed atoms are divisible and contain internal structure.

    4. Ernest Rutherford

    • (1871–1937) – Nuclear Model of the Atom
    • Who: New Zealand-born British physicist
    • Contribution: Conducted the gold foil experiment (1909).
    • Discovery: Found that most alpha particles passed through foil, but some deflected sharply.
    • Conclusion: Atoms have a dense, positively charged nucleus, with electrons orbiting around it.
    • Model: Introduced the nuclear model, replacing Thomson’s.

    Called the “father of nuclear physics,” Rutherford also discovered the proton.

    5. Niels Bohr

    • (1885–1962) – Quantized Electron Orbits
    • Who: Danish physicist
    • Contribution: Modified Rutherford’s model to explain atomic spectra.
    • Key Idea: Electrons occupy discrete energy levels (orbits), and light is emitted/absorbed when electrons change levels.
    • Model: The Bohr Model (1913), particularly successful for hydrogen.

    6. Erwin Schrödinger

    • (1887–1961) – Quantum Mechanical Model
    • Who: Austrian physicist
    • Contribution: Developed the Schrödinger equation, treating electrons as wave-like.
    • Model: Replaced Bohr’s orbits with orbitals—regions where electrons are likely to be found.
    • Impact: Laid the foundation for modern quantum mechanics.

    7. Werner Heisenberg

    • (1901–1976) – Uncertainty Principle
    • Who: German physicist
    • Contribution: Formulated the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, stating we cannot precisely know both the position and momentum of an electron.
    • Significance: Undermined the idea of fixed orbits and supported the probabilistic nature of quantum theory.

    8. James Chadwick

    • (1891–1974) – Discovery of the Neutron
    • Who: British physicist
    • Contribution: Discovered the neutron in 1932, completing the basic picture of atomic structure.
    • Impact: Explained atomic mass discrepancies and enabled understanding of nuclear reactions.

    9. Quantum Mechanical Model

    • (1920s–present)
    • Introduced by Schrödinger, Heisenberg, and others.
    • Electrons are described as wave-like entities in probability clouds or orbitals, not fixed paths.
    • Heisenberg’s Uncertainty Principle states that the exact position and momentum of an electron cannot be simultaneously known.
