    Fictitious Force

    A fictitious force (or inertial force) is a force that appears to act on a mass when observed from a non-inertial frame (a frame that is accelerating or rotating relative to an inertial frame). These forces do not arise from any physical interaction but instead result from the acceleration of the reference frame itself.

    Formula

    • Fictitious Force: For an observer in a non-inertial frame with acceleration $a$, the fictitious force $\vec F$ on a mass $m$ is: $$\vec F=-m\vec a$$

    Symbols

    • $\vec F\quad$ Fictitious Force: A force that appears due to the acceleration of the reference frame, not due to physical interactions.
    • $m\quad$ Mass: The mass of the object experiencing the fictitious force.
    • $\vec a\quad$ Acceleration of the Reference Frame: The acceleration of the non-inertial frame relative to an inertial one.

    Explanation:

    • This force is not a real interaction but is introduced to account for the acceleration of the reference frame.
    • It allows Newtons second law to be applied as if the frame were inertial.

    Examples of Fictitious Forces:

    • Centrifugal Force: Appears to push objects outward in a rotating reference frame.
    • Coriolis Force: Affects moving objects in a rotating frame, causing a deflection (e.g., weather patterns on Earth).
    • Euler Force: Occurs when the angular velocity of a rotating frame changes.
    • Linear Inertial Force: A force experienced in a linearly accelerating frame (e.g., feeling pushed back in an accelerating car).
