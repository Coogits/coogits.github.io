    Electron

    An electron is a subatomic particle with a negative charge. It is one of the fundamental components of an atom and plays a key role in chemical bonding, electricity, and many other physical phenomena.
    Electrons are found in orbitals around the nucleus of an atom.

    Charge and Mass

    • Charge: Negative (-1e ≈ -1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
    • Mass: Approximately 9.109 × 10⁻³¹ kg, or 5.4858 × 10⁻⁴ amu
      • The mass of an electron is about 1/1836 of that of a proton, making it the lightest of the three main subatomic particles.

    Electron Configuration

    Electron configuration in atoms determines their chemical properties and their ability to form bonds.

    Octet Rule

    Atom tendency to achieving a stable electron configuration similar to that of the noble gases.

    Quantum Number

    1. Principal Quantum Number (n)
    2. Azimuthal Quantum Number (ℓ)
    3. Magnetic Quantum Number (mₗ)
    4. Spin Quantum Number (mₛ)

    Energy Level

    Electrons are arranged in energy levels (also known as electron shells or orbitals) around the nucleus, with specific energy states.

    Electron Shell

    Orbital

    Valence Electrons

    Valence electrons are the outermost electrons of an atom, located in the highest energy level (shell) that is partially or fully occupied.
    These electrons are primarily responsible for an atom’s chemical properties and its ability to form bonds with other atoms.

    Role in the Atom

    • In a neutral atom, the number of electrons equals the number of protons, ensuring no net charge.

    Movement and Energy Levels

    • Electrons move in probabilistic regions around the nucleus known as orbitals, not fixed orbits. This concept is central to quantum mechanics.
    • The energy levels (or shells) of electrons are quantized, meaning they can only occupy specific energy states. When electrons transition between these states, they absorb or emit energy in the form of photons (light).

    Properties and Behavior

    • Electrons exhibit both particle and wave-like behavior, a dual nature described by wave-particle duality in quantum mechanics.
    • They obey the Heisenberg Uncertainty Principle, meaning that the position and momentum of an electron cannot be simultaneously measured with perfect accuracy.

    Historical Note

    • The electron was discovered in 1897 by J.J. Thomson using cathode ray tube experiments.
    • The discovery was pivotal in understanding atomic structure and led to the development of the Plum Pudding Model and later, more advanced atomic models.
