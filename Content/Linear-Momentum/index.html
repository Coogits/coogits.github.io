    Linear Momentum

    Momentum (often referred to as linear momentum) is a physical quantity that represents the motion of an object. It is defined as the product of an object's mass and its velocity. Mathematically.
    Momentum is a vector quantity, meaning it has both magnitude and direction. It is conserved in isolated systems where no external forces act, according to the principle of conservation of momentum, which is a fundamental concept in both classical mechanics and modern physics.

    Symbols

    • $\vec p\quad$ is the Momentum of the object (unit: $\cfrac{kg\cdot m}{s}$)
    • $m \quad$ is the Inertial Mass of the object (in kilograms),
    • $\vec v\quad$ is the Velocity of the object (in meters per second).

    Formulas

    • Linear Momentum Definition:
      is defined as the product of an object's mass and its velocity: $$\vec{p}=m\vec{v}$$ Since velocity is the Derivative of displacement with respect to time: $$\vec p=m\cfrac{ds}{dt}$$

    SI Unit

    • the SI Unit is the kilogram meter per second: $$\vec p=1\cfrac{kg\cdot m}{s}$$

    Properties

    • Redefinition of the Newton's second law: $$\vec{F}=\cfrac{d\vec{p}}{dt}$$
    • Superposition Principle: $$\vec{p}_{tot}=\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n\vec{p}_i$$

    Principle of Momentum Conservation

    • States that the total momentum of an isolated system remains constant if no external forces act on it. This principle applies in both elastic and inelastic collisions and is a direct consequence of Newton’s third law (action-reaction forces being equal and opposite). Mathematically, this is expressed as: $$\sum p_{\text{initial}}=\sum p_{\text{final}}$$

    Impulse-Momentum Theorem

    • The Impulse-Momentum Theorem highlights the relationship between the force applied to an object, the time it acts upon that object, and the resulting change in the object's motion: $$\vec J=\Delta \vec p$$
