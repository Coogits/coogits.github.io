    Kinetic Energy

    Kinetic energy is the energy an object possesses due to its motion. The amount of kinetic energy depends on the mass of the object and its velocity. It is a scalar quantity and is always positive, as both mass and velocity are non-negative.

    Symbols

    • $K_t\quad$ Translational Kinetic Energy; the kinetic energy due to the linear motion of an object, measured in Joules (J).
    • $K_r\quad$ Rotational Kinetic Energy; the kinetic energy due to the rotational motion of an object, measured in Joules (J).
    • $K\quad$ Total Kinetic Energy; is the total kinetic energy of an object, combining both translational and rotational kinetic energy, in Joules (J).
    • $m\quad$ Inertial Mass; is the mass of the object, a measure of its resistance to acceleration, in kilograms (kg).
    • $\vec v\quad$ Velocity; is the velocity vector of the object, describing both its speed and direction, in meters per second (m/s).
    • $I\quad$ Moment Of Inertia; is the rotational equivalent of mass, describing an object's resistance to changes in its rotational motion, in kilogram meters squared ($\text{kg}\cdot m^2$)
    • $\omega\quad$ Angular Velocity; is the rate of change of angular displacement, describing the speed of rotation, in radians per second ($\cfrac{\text{rad}}{s}$).
    • $v\quad$ Speed; is the magnitude of the velocity vector, describing how fast an object is moving, in meters per second (m\s)

    Formulas

    • Translational Kinetic Energy:
      describes the energy due to the motion of an object in a straight line: $$K_t=\frac{1}{2}m||\vec{v}||^2$$
    • Rotational Kinetic Energy:
      refers to the energy of an object due to its rotation about an axis: $$K_r=\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$$
    • Total Kinetic Energy:
      the total kinetic energy is the sum of the translational and rotational kinetic energy: $$K=K_t+K_r=\underbrace{\frac{1}{2}mv^2}_t+\underbrace{\frac{1}{2}I\omega^2}_r$$

    SI Unit

    • SI Unit: The SI Unit is the Joule ($1J$): $$E=1J=1N\cdot m=1kg\cdot m^2\cdot s^{-2}$$

    Properties

    • Velocity squared:
      Since velocity appears squared in the formula, small increases in velocity lead to large increases in kinetic energy. For instance, doubling the speed of an object will quadruple its kinetic energy.
    • Mass dependence:
      Heavier objects have more kinetic energy at the same speed compared to lighter objects.
    • Mechanical Energy's component:
      Its a component of the Mechanical Energy $E=K+U$
    • Non-Conservation of Kinetic Energy:
      Unlike mechanical energy, kinetic energy is not always conserved in an isolated system.
      • Kinetic energy can transform into potential energy while the total mechanical energy remains constant.
      • This occurs in systems where energy shifts between motion (kinetic) and stored energy (potential), such as a pendulum swing or a falling object.
      • However, in inelastic collisions, kinetic energy is not conserved because some energy is converted into heat, sound, or deformation.
