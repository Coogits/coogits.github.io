    Logarithm

    The logarithm operator is the inverse operation of exponentiation. It determines the exponent to which a given base must be raised to produce a specified number.

    Symbols

    • $a\quad$ Base of the logarithm,
    • $x\quad$ Argument
    • $y\quad$ Exponent that satisfies the equation

    Formulas

    • Definition
      Is defined as: $$\log_a(x)=y$$ where $$a>0;a\neq1$$

    Properties

    • Inverse of Exponentiation:
      The logarithm and exponentiation are inverse operations:
      • When the base appears as the argument: $$\log_a(a^x)=x$$
      • When the base is raised to the logarithm: $$a^{log_a^x}=x$$
    • Product Rule:
      log of a product is the sum of logs: $$\log_a(xy)=\log_ax+\log_ay$$
    • Quotient Rule:
      log of a fraction is the difference of logs: $$\log_a\left(\frac{x}{y}\right)=\log_ax-\log_ay$$
    • Power Rule:
      log of an exponentiated value is the exponent times the log: $$\log_a(x^r)=r\log_ax$$
    • Change of Base:
      The logarithm of x with base a can be rewritten using a different base b:$$\log_ax=\cfrac{\log_bx}{\log_ba}$$ where:
      • $a\quad$ is the original base
      • $b\quad$ is the new base (any valid logarithm base, typically 10 or e)

    Natural Logarithm

    The natural logarithm (denoted as $\ln x$) is the logarithm with base $( e )$, where $e \approx 2.718$:

    Key Concepts

    • Base:
      The natural logarithm is a special case of logarithm with base e $$\ln x = \log_e x$$
    • Properties
      It follows all standard logarithm properties.
    • Uses:
      Commonly used in calculus, exponential growth/decay, and probability theory.
    • Derivative:
      Its derivative is: $$\frac{d}{dx} \ln x = \frac{1}{x}$$

    Common Logarithm

    The common logarithm (denoted as $\log x$) is the logarithm with base 10.

    Key Concepts

    • Base:
      The common logarithm is a special case of logarithm with base 10 $$\log x = \log_{10} x$$
    • Properties
      It follows all standard logarithm properties.
    • Uses:
      Widely used in engineering, scientific notation, and logarithmic scales (e.g., decibels, pH).
      Often used in computations before calculators supported other bases.
