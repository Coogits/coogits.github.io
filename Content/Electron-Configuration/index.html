    Electron Configuration

    Electron configuration refers to the arrangement of electrons in the orbitals of an atom.
    Electrons are distributed in various energy levels or shells, and within each shell, they occupy orbitals according to specific rules, such as the Aufbau principle, the Pauli exclusion principle, and Hund's rule. This configuration determines an element’s chemical properties and its reactivity.

    Terminology

    Context Preferred Term(s) Why
    Basic Chemistry Shell, Energy Level Simple, intuitive
    Bonding or Valence Electrons Shell, Subshell Helps explain outer electrons
    Electron Configurations Subshell, Orbital You need to say things like 1s² 2s² 2p⁶
    Quantum Mechanics Quantum Numbers, Orbitals Describes position, spin, etc. in detail
    Visuals or Diagrams Shell, Orbital Easy to map conceptually

    Octet Rule

    Atoms tend to gain, lose, or share electrons in order to have eight electrons in their valence (outermost) shell, achieving a stable electron configuration similar to that of the noble gases.

    Quantum Number

    1. Principal Quantum Number (n)

    • Indicates the main energy level (or "shell").
    • Values: $n=\{1,2,\ldots,+\infty\}$
    • Can be represented as numbers or characters:
      • n = 1 = K
      • n = 2 = L
      • n = 3 = M
      • n = 4 = N
    • Higher n means higher energy and farther from the nucleus.

    2. Azimuthal Quantum Number (ℓ)

    • Describes the subshell or orbital shape.
    • Values: $\ell=\{0,1,\ldots,n-1\}$
    • Corresponds to:
      • ℓ = 0 → s orbital
      • ℓ = 1 → p orbital
      • ℓ = 2 → d orbital
      • ℓ = 3 → f orbital

    3. Magnetic Quantum Number (mₗ)

    • Specifies the orientation of the orbital in space.
    • Values: $m_l=\{-\ell,0,+\ell\}$
    • Determines the number of orbitals in each subshell.
      • s (ℓ=0) → 1 orbital
      • p (ℓ=1) → 3 orbitals
      • d (ℓ=2) → 5 orbitals
      • f (ℓ=3) → 5 orbitals

    4. Spin Quantum Number (mₛ)

    • Describes the spin of the electron.
    • Values: $m_s=\{-\frac{1}{2},+\frac{1}{2}\}$
    • No two electrons in the same atom can have the same four quantum numbers (Pauli Exclusion Principle).
    • Due to the Pauli Principle, each orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons with opposite spins.

    Notation

    Electron configuration is typically written by listing the energy levels, subshells, and the number of electrons in each subshell. For example:

    • 1s² 2s² 2p⁶ 3s² 3p⁶ (for Neon, Ne) indicates the electron configuration for Neon, where:
      • The first energy level (1s) holds 2 electrons.
      • The second energy level has 2 electrons in the 2s subshell and 6 electrons in the 2p subshell.
      • The third energy level has 2 electrons in the 3s subshell and 6 electrons in the 3p subshell. For elements beyond Neon, electron configurations will continue filling orbitals in the next available energy levels.

    Graphical Representation

    The electron configuration can also be represented graphically using orbital diagrams, where: $$ \textbf{Shell 3: } \begin{array}{|c|} \hline \uparrow\downarrow\\ \hline \end{array} \quad \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline \uparrow\downarrow & \uparrow\downarrow & \uparrow\downarrow \\ \hline \end{array} \quad \begin{array}{|c|c|c|c|c|} \hline \uparrow\downarrow & \uparrow\downarrow & \uparrow\downarrow & \uparrow\downarrow & \uparrow\downarrow \\ \hline \end{array} $$ Each box represents an orbital, and arrows indicate electrons:

    • $\uparrow\quad$ Represent the Electron (Atom) with Spin UP
    • $\downarrow\quad$ Represent the Electron (Atom) with Spin Down
    • Box with arrows : An orbital, which can hold up to two electrons (opposite spins)
    • Grouped boxes : A subshell —
      • 1 box for s subshell (1 orbital)
      • 3 boxes for p subshell (3 orbitals)
      • 5 boxes for d subshell (5 orbitals)
      • 7 boxes for f subshell (7 orbitals)
    • Line of grouped boxes: Represent the Energy Level or Shell

    Principles

    • Hund's Rule:
      When electrons occupy degenerate orbitals (orbitals of the same energy), they will first fill them singly with parallel spins before pairing up. $$ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline \uparrow\ & \uparrow\ & \uparrow\ \\ \hline \end{array} $$ Wrong: $$ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline \uparrow\downarrow & \ \ & \ \ \\ \hline \end{array} $$

    • Pauli Exclusion Principle:
      No two electrons in the same atom can have identical sets of quantum numbers. This means each orbital can hold a maximum of two electrons with opposite spins. $$ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline \uparrow\downarrow & \uparrow\ & \uparrow\ \\ \hline \end{array} $$ Wrong: $$ \begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline \uparrow\uparrow & \uparrow\ & \uparrow\ \\ \hline \end{array} $$

    • Aufbau Principle:
      Electrons fill orbitals starting from the lowest energy level and move to higher energy levels as needed. $$1s → 2s → 2p → 3s → 3p → 4s → 3d → 4p → 5s → 4d → 5p → 6s → 4f → 5d → 6p → 7s\ldots$$

    Example: Electron Configuration of Carbon (C)

    • Carbon has an atomic number of 6, meaning it has 6 electrons. Its electron configuration is:
      • 1s² 2s² 2p²
        The first energy level (1s) holds 2 electrons, the second energy level (2s) holds 2 electrons, and the remaining 2 electrons are placed in the 2p subshell.

    Importance of Electron Configuration

    • Chemical Behavior:
      The electron configuration determines how atoms interact in chemical reactions. The electrons in the outermost shell, called valence electrons, are particularly important in bonding and chemical reactions.
    • Periodic Table Organization:
      The electron configuration explains the arrangement of elements in the Periodic Table. Elements in the same group have similar electron configurations, especially in their outermost shells, leading to similar chemical properties.
