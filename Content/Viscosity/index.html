    Viscosity and Shear Stress

    Viscosity is a measure of a fluid's resistance to deformation or flow. It quantifies the internal friction between adjacent layers of fluid moving at different velocities. This resistance manifests as a shear stress ($\tau$), which is proportional to the velocity gradient in the fluid, according to Newtons Law of Viscosity. A fluid with high viscosity (e.g., honey) resists motion more than a fluid with low viscosity (e.g., water).

    Formulas

    • Dynamic Viscosity: Mathematically, viscosity is described by Newton's Law of Viscosity: $$\tau=\mu\cfrac{du}{dy}$$
    • Kinematic Viscosity: describes how easily a fluid flows under gravity alone: $$v=\cfrac{\mu}{\rho}$$

    Symbols

    • $\tau\quad$ is the shear stress, force per unit area due to viscosity (units: Pa)
    • $\mu\quad$ is the dynamic viscosity, measures a fluids internal resistance to flow, (units: $Pa\cdot s$)
    • $\cfrac{du}{dy}\quad$ is the velocity gradient perpendicular to the flow direction.
    • $v\quad$ Kinematic viscosity, describes how easily a fluid flows under gravity alone (units: $\cfrac{m^2}{s}$)
    • $\rho\quad$ Density (Physics), represents the mass per unit volume of the fluid (units: $\cfrac{kg}{m^3}$).

    Properties

    • In gases, viscosity increases as temperature increases. This happens because higher temperatures lead to more molecular collisions, which increase resistance to flow.
    • In liquids, viscosity decreases as temperature increases. This occurs because higher temperatures weaken intermolecular forces, making the liquid flow more easily.
