    Archimedes' Principle

    Archimedes' Principle states that any object submerged in a fluid (either fully or partially) experiences an upward buoyant force equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. This force acts in the opposite direction of gravity and is responsible for the phenomenon of buoyancy. The principle is fundamental in understanding why objects float or sink in fluids.

    Formulas

    • Buoyant Force: The upward force exerted by a fluid on a submerged object, given by: $$F_b=\rho_f{V}g$$
    • Buoyant Force (Explanation): According to Archimedes' Principle, this force is equal to the weight of the displaced fluid: $$F_b=F_w=mg=\rho_f{V}g$$

    Symbols

    • $F_b\quad$ is the buoyant force.
    • $\rho_f\quad$ is the Density (Physics) of the fluid.
    • $V\quad$ is the volume of fluid displaced by the object.
    • $g\quad$ is the Gravitational Acceleration
    • $F_w\quad$ is the Weight (Force) of the displaced fluid (or the object itself when floating),
    • $m\quad$ is the Gravitational Mass of the displaced fluid (or the object),

    Properties

    • Negligible Buoyant Force Without Weight: In the absence of Weight (Force) (gravitational force), the Archimedean buoyant force is negligible.
    • Dependence on Displaced Volume: The buoyant force depends only on the volume of fluid displaced, the fluid's density, and the gravitational acceleration, not on the shape or the material of the object.
    • Direction: The buoyant force always acts upward, opposing the gravitational force.
    • Floating and Sinking: An object will float if its weight is less than or equal to the buoyant force, and sink if its weight exceeds the buoyant force.
    • Equal to Weight of Displaced Fluid: According to Archimedes' Principle, the buoyant force is equal to the weight of the fluid displaced by the object.
