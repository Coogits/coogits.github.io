    Scientific Revolution

    The Scientific Revolution (16th to 18th century) was a profound period of change in human understanding of the natural world. It marked the shift from medieval and religious explanations to observation-based, empirical science, laying the groundwork for modern scientific methods.

    • Start: 1543 CE
    • End: 1687 CE

    Timeline

    Key Features

    • Empirical Approach: The Scientific Revolution emphasized observation, experimentation, and the scientific method as opposed to relying on ancient authorities or religious doctrine.
    • Challenging Traditional Views: It questioned long-standing beliefs from ancient thinkers like Aristotle and the Church, particularly in fields such as astronomy, physics, and medicine.

    Major Discoveries

    • Astronomy:
      • Nicolaus Copernicus introduced the heliocentric model (Sun at the center, not Earth), which challenged the geocentric view of the universe.
      • Galileo Galilei used the telescope to observe celestial bodies, providing evidence that supported Copernicus' theory.
      • Johannes Kepler discovered the laws of planetary motion, showing planets orbit in ellipses, not circles.
    • Physics:
      • Isaac Newton unified earlier findings into his laws of motion and theory of gravity, explaining planetary movement and laying the foundation for classical mechanics.
    • Medicine and Anatomy:
      • Andreas Vesalius conducted detailed dissections and corrected many of Galen’s anatomical inaccuracies.
      • William Harvey demonstrated the circulation of blood in the body, revolutionizing the understanding of human physiology.

    Scientific Method

    • Promoted by figures like Francis Bacon and René Descartes, the scientific method emphasized hypothesis, controlled experiments, and the use of reason and evidence over tradition. This approach reshaped how scientific knowledge was obtained and tested.

    Impact

    • The Scientific Revolution sparked the development of modern science, influencing many fields such as physics, astronomy, chemistry, and biology.
    • It laid the intellectual foundation for the Enlightenment, where reason and empirical knowledge further shaped philosophical and political thought.
    • It also led to changes in societal structures, as scientific progress increasingly influenced technological advances, medicine, and everyday life.
