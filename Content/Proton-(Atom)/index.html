    Proton

    A proton is a positively charged subatomic particle found in the nucleus of every atom.
    It is one of the primary building blocks of atomic structure, along with neutrons and electrons. Protons define the identity of an element via the atomic number.

    Charge and Mass

    • Elementary Charge: +1 elementary charge (+1e ≈ +1.602 × 10⁻¹⁹ C)
    • Mass: Approximately 1.6726 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, or 1.007276 amu
      • Protons are roughly 1836 times more massive than electrons.

    Role in the Atom

    • Located in the nucleus, protons contribute significantly to an atom’s mass and participate in nuclear forces.
    • The number of protons in the nucleus determines the atomic number (Z) of the element.
      • For example, Carbon has 6 protons → atomic number = 6.
    • In a neutral atom, the number of protons equals the number of electrons.

    Chemistry

    In chemistry, the proton is fundamental:

    • Element: Defined based on the number of protons in its atoms.
    • Atomic Number: Represents the number of protons in an element and determines its identity.
    • Mass Number: The protons, together with neutrons, contribute to the mass number of an element.

    Internal Structure

    Protons are not fundamental particles. They are composed of three quarks:

    • Two up quarks (u)
    • One down quark (d) These quarks are bound together by the strong nuclear force, mediated by particles called gluons. This structure classifies protons as baryons, a type of hadron.

    Stability and Lifespan

    • Protons are considered stable in ordinary matter.
    • Theoretical physics suggests protons may decay over immense timescales (>10³⁴ years), but proton decay has never been observed experimentally.

    Historical Note

    • The proton was discovered in 1917 by Ernest Rutherford, who observed hydrogen nuclei being emitted from nitrogen during alpha particle bombardment.
    • The name "proton" was adopted later, derived from the Greek protos, meaning "first."
