    Bronze Age

    The Bronze Age was a period in human history when people began using bronze (a metal alloy made of copper and tin) to create tools, weapons, and artifacts. It followed the Neolithic Age and marked the rise of the first major civilizations.

    • Start: c. 3,000 BCE
    • End: 1200 BCE

    Timeline

    Civilizations

    Ancient Egypt

    • Start: c. 3,100 BCE
    • End: 30 BCE (Roman conquest)
    • Key Features: The rise of pharaohs, the building of pyramids, and advances in mathematics, astronomy, and medicine.

    Greek Bronze Age

    • Start: c. 3,000 BCE
    • End: 1,100 BCE
    • Key Features: The emergence of the Minoan and Mycenaean civilizations, early Greek writing systems like Linear A and Linear B, and the development of trade across the Mediterranean.

    Mesopotamian Civilization

    • Start: c. 3,500 BCE
    • End: 1,500 BCE (Transition to the Iron Age)
    • Key Features: The rise of the Sumerians, the development of cuneiform writing, the construction of ziggurats, and the establishment of city-states like Ur and Babylon.

    Babylonia

    • Start: c. 1,850 BCE
    • End: 539 BCE (Persian conquest)
    • Key Features: The creation of the Code of Hammurabi, the rise of Babylon as a major cultural and political center, and advancements in mathematics, astronomy, and law.

    Key Features

    1. Metalworking & Tools:
      • People developed advanced bronze tools, weapons, and armor, which were stronger than stone.
      • The use of casting techniques allowed for mass production of metal items.
    2. Rise of Cities & Civilizations:
      • The first urban civilizations emerged, including:
        • Mesopotamia (Sumer, Akkad, Babylon)
        • Ancient Egypt
        • Indus Valley Civilization
        • Shang Dynasty (China)
      • These societies had organized governments, social hierarchies, and trade networks.
    3. Writing & Record Keeping:
      • The earliest writing systems developed, including:
        • Cuneiform (Mesopotamia)
        • Hieroglyphics (Egypt)
      • Writing was used for laws, trade, and religious texts.
    4. Agriculture & Trade:
      • Large-scale irrigation systems improved farming.
      • Trade networks connected civilizations, spreading bronze, pottery, textiles, and luxury goods.
    5. Architecture & Monuments:
      • Great structures like the Ziggurats of Mesopotamia, Pyramids of Egypt, and Stonehenge were built.
    6. Religion & Mythology:
      • Polytheistic religions flourished, with gods controlling aspects of nature and society.
      • Priests and rulers often worked together to maintain power.

    End of the Bronze Age

    Around 1,200 BCE, the Bronze Age ended due to a combination of natural disasters, invasions (Sea Peoples), and economic collapse. This led to the Iron Age, where iron replaced bronze as the dominant metal for tools and weapons.
