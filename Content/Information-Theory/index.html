    Information Theory

    Information Theory is a branch of computer science and mathematics that studies the quantification, storage, and communication of information. It provides a theoretical framework for understanding concepts such as data compression, transmission efficiency, and error detection/correction in digital and analog systems.

    Key Concepts

    • Binary Digit (Bit):
      The fundamental unit of information, representing a 0 or 1 in binary systems.

    • Entropy:
      Measures the average amount of information (or uncertainty) in a data source. Higher entropy means more unpredictability. Entropy is measured in bits, quantifying how much information is needed to represent data.

    • Encoding:
      Converts data into a specific format for storage, transmission, or compression. Essential for data representation and communication.

    • Data Compression:
      Reduces the amount of data needed to represent information. Includes lossless (Huffman coding, LZW) and lossy (JPEG, MP3) techniques.

    • Channel Capacity:
      The maximum rate at which information can be reliably transmitted over a communication channel, as defined by Shannonâ€™s theorem.

    • Error-Correcting Codes: Methods to detect and correct errors in transmitted or stored data (e.g., Hamming code, Reed-Solomon).

    • Mutual Information:
      Measures how much information two systems share. Higher values indicate stronger relationships between variables.
