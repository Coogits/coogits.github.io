    Electromagnetic Wave

    An electromagnetic wave is a form of energy transmission that consists of oscillating electric (E) and magnetic (B) fields, which are mutually perpendicular and propagate through space as a transverse wave. Unlike mechanical waves, electromagnetic waves do not require a medium and can travel through a vacuum at the speed of light (c ≈ 3.00 × 10⁸ m/s).

    Symbols

    • $\varepsilon_0\quad$ Vacuum Permittivity
    • $\mu_0\quad$ Vacuum Permeability

    Formulas

    Speed of Propagation

    • Speed of Propagation In a vacuum:
      In a vacuum, electromagnetic waves travel at the speed of light: $$c=\cfrac{1}{\varepsilon_0\mu_0}$$
    • Speed of Propagation In a vacuum:
      In a medium, the speed is reduced based on the material's permittivity $\varepsilon$ and permeability $\mu$: $$v=\cfrac{c}{n}$$ where $n$ is the refractive index of the medium.

    Frequency and Wavelength

    • Frequency:
      Number of complete cycles (oscillations) per unit of time, in hertz (Hz), where 1 Hz = 1 cycle per second. $$f=\cfrac{1}{T}$$
    • Wavelength:
      Physical length of one complete cycle, in meters (m). It is inversely related to frequency: $$\lambda=\cfrac{c}{f}$$

    Electromagnetic Spectrum

    Electromagnetic waves span a broad range of frequencies and wavelengths, classified as follows:

    Type of Wave Frequency Range Wavelength Range Applications
    Radio Waves <3 GHz >10 cm Broadcasting, Wi-Fi, Bluetooth
    Microwaves 300 MHz – 300 GHz 1 mm – 1 m Radar, Satellite, Microwave Ovens
    Infrared (IR) 300 GHz – 430 THz 700 nm – 1 mm Remote Controls, Thermal Imaging
    Visible Light 430 THz – 770 THz 400 nm – 700 nm Human Vision, Optical Fibers
    Ultraviolet (UV) 770 THz – 303 PHz 10 nm – 400 nm Sterilization, Sunlight Exposure
    X-Rays 30 PHz – 30 EHz 0.01 nm – 10 nm Medical Imaging, Security Scanning
    Gamma Rays > 30 EHz <0.01 nm Nuclear Reactions, Cancer Treatment

    Generation and Propagation

    Electromagnetic waves are produced by accelerating charges, such as:

    • Oscillating dipoles (e.g., antennas in radio transmission).
    • Quantum transitions (e.g., photons emitted from atomic energy level changes).
    • Thermal radiation (e.g., infrared radiation from a heated object).

    Propagation depends on environmental conditions:

    • Reflection – Wave bounces off a surface (e.g., mirrors, radar).
    • Refraction – Wave bends when moving between media of different refractive indices.
    • Diffraction – Wave spreads around obstacles or through openings.
    • Scattering – Wave interacts with small particles (e.g., Rayleigh scattering causes the blue sky).

    Telecommunications

    In telecommunications, electromagnetic waves are the medium for wireless signal transmission. The choice of frequency band affects range, penetration, and data capacity:

    • Radio and microwaves are used in Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, satellite, and cellular networks.
    • Infrared is used in remote controls and short-range communication.
    • Optical waves (visible light and IR) are used in fiber-optic communication.
