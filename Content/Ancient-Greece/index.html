    Ancient Greece

    Ancient Greece was one of the most influential civilizations in history, known for its philosophy, democracy, art, literature, and military achievements. It laid the foundations of Western civilization and significantly shaped modern politics, science, and culture.

    • Start: c. 3000 BCE
    • End: 146 BCE

    Timeline

    Key Periods

    Greek Bronze Age

    (c. 3000–1100 BCE)

    • Minoan Civilization (c. 3000–1450 BCE)
      • Flourishing on the island of Crete.
      • Known for palaces like Knossos and advanced art and architecture.
      • Linear A script (undeciphered).
      • Ends around 1450 BCE, possibly due to natural disasters or Mycenaean invasions.
    • Mycenaean Civilization (c. 1600–1100 BCE)
      • First Greek-speaking civilization.
      • Focused on fortified palaces (e.g., Mycenae, Pylos, Tiryns).
      • Linear B script (early form of Greek writing).
      • Declines around 1100 BCE due to invasions, likely by the Dorians or Sea Peoples.

    Greek Dark Ages

    (c. 1100–800 BCE)

    • After the collapse of Mycenaean civilization, Greek culture goes into a period of decline with no major written records.
    • Small tribal communities dominate, and there are few major urban centers.
    • Trade and cultural activity decline significantly, but the Phoenician alphabet is adopted.
    • Ends when the Greeks begin to emerge from the darkness around 800 BCE, leading into the Archaic Period.

    Greek Archaic Period

    (c. 800–500 BCE)

    • Rise of City-States (Poleis): The Greek city-states (e.g., Athens, Sparta, Corinth) emerge and begin to form.
    • Colonization: Greeks establish colonies in Italy, Sicily, Asia Minor, and the Black Sea.
    • Political Developments: Introduction of early forms of democracy (e.g., Solon and Cleisthenes in Athens), the establishment of oligarchies and tyrannies.
    • Literature and Art: Homer writes the Iliad and Odyssey, and the first Greek sculpture begins to appear.
    • End of the Archaic Period: The period ends around 500 BCE, when the Persian Wars begin.

    Greek Classical Period

    (c. 500–323 BCE)

    • Persian Wars (499–449 BCE): The Greek city-states, including Athens and Sparta, unite against the Persian Empire. Key battles include Marathon (490 BCE), Salamis (480 BCE), and Plataea (479 BCE).
    • Golden Age of Athens:
      • Pericles strengthens Athenian democracy and builds the Parthenon.
      • Flourishing of philosophy (e.g., Socrates, Plato, Aristotle), art, and theatre (e.g., Sophocles, Euripides).
      • Major intellectual and cultural achievements, including the first philosophers and dramatic plays.
    • Peloponnesian War (431–404 BCE): A long and devastating war between Athens and Sparta, which ends in Spartan victory but weakens all of Greece.
    • Macedonian Expansion: Philip II of Macedon unifies Greece, setting the stage for Alexander the Great.
    • Death of Alexander (323 BCE): The death of Alexander the Great marks the end of the Classical Period.

    Greek Hellenistic Period

    (323–146 BCE)

    • Following Alexander the Great's death, his empire is divided into three major Hellenistic kingdoms:
      • Ptolemaic Egypt (under Ptolemy I).
      • Seleucid Empire (spanning Asia).
      • Antigonid Macedonia (under Antigonus).
    • Greek culture spreads far beyond Greece itself, including to Egypt, Persia, and India, resulting in a fusion of Greek and local cultures (e.g., Hellenistic art, Hellenistic philosophy, and scientific advances).
    • End of Hellenistic Greece: Greece falls under Roman control after the Battle of Corinth in 146 BCE, marking the end of Greek independence.

    Key Aspects

    Government & Democracy

    • Athens developed the first democracy (rule by the people).
    • Other city-states had different systems: Sparta was a military oligarchy.

    Philosophy & Science

    • Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle shaped Western philosophy.
    • Pythagoras (mathematics)
    • Hippocrates (father of medicine) and Archimedes (mathematics & engineering).

    Art & Architecture

    • Built temples (Parthenon), statues, and amphitheaters.
    • Developed classical sculpture, pottery, and frescoes.

    Religion & Mythology

    • Worshiped gods like Zeus, Athena, Apollo, and Poseidon.
    • Believed in oracles (Delphi) and heroic myths (Hercules, Achilles, Odysseus).

    Military Achievements

    • Phalanx formation revolutionized warfare.
    • Spartan warriors were among the most feared soldiers in history.

    Legacy of Ancient Greece:

    • Inspired modern democracy, philosophy, literature, and science.

    • Greek ideas influenced Rome and later European civilizations.

    • Many Greek texts were preserved and studied during the Renaissance.
