    Degree of Freedom (Physical System)

    The degree of freedom (DOF) of a Physical System refers to the number of independent parameters or coordinates required to fully describe the state or configuration of the system. It essentially quantifies how many independent ways the components of the system can move or change without violating any constraints imposed on the system.

    Formulas

    • Notation:
      denoted by the symbol $f$ or $v$: $$\textbf{DOF}=f$$

    Examples

    • A material point in $\mathbb{R}$ have 1 DOF (translational)
    • A material point in $\mathbb{R}^2$ have 2 DOF (translational)
    • A material point in $\mathbb{R}^3$ have 3 DOF (translational)
    • A rigid body in $\mathbb{R}^2$ have 3 DOF (2 translational and 1 rotational)
    • A rigid body in $\mathbb{R}^3$ have 6 DOF (3 translational and 3 rotational)

    Degrees of freedom in different contexts:

    • In classical mechanics, the number of degrees of freedom helps determine the system's configuration and the number of independent coordinates required to describe its position.
    • In thermodynamics, the concept of degrees of freedom is used to define the number of independent variables that can be specified without violating the constraints (for example, in the context of the phase rule for gases and liquids).
