    Physical Layer

    The Physical Layer (Layer 1) of the OSI model is responsible for the transmission and reception of raw data bits over a physical medium. This layer deals with the hardware aspects of communication, such as the cables, switches, network interface cards, electrical signals, optical signals, and radio waves that physically transmit data between devices.

    Electromagnetic Wave

    In telecommunications, electromagnetic waves serve as the medium for wireless signal transmission. The frequency band used impacts range, penetration, and data capacity:

    • Radio and microwaves: Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, satellite, and cellular networks.
    • Infrared: Remote controls and short-range communication.
    • Optical waves: Fiber-optic communication.

    Magnetic Permeability

    Magnetic permeability affects:

    • Electromagnetic wave propagation: Influences interaction with radio and microwave signals.
    • Transformer and inductor design: Materials with high permeability enhance magnetic field storage.
    • Antenna design: Affects impedance matching and shielding.

    Wave Frequency

    • Definition: The number of oscillations per second, measured in hertz (Hz).
    • Telecommunications Role: Higher frequencies allow faster data transmission, but lower frequencies penetrate obstacles better.
      • Low frequencies: Long-range, better penetration (e.g., AM radio).
      • High frequencies: Higher data capacity, shorter range (e.g., microwave, millimeter waves for 5G).

    Wavelength

    • Definition: The length of one complete cycle, inversely related to frequency.
    • Telecommunications Role: Affects propagation and obstacle penetration.
      • Longer wavelengths: Better for long-range and penetration (e.g., radio waves).
      • Shorter wavelengths: Suited for high-speed transmission (e.g., fiber-optic, high-frequency applications).

    Wave Amplitude

    Amplitude refers to the maximum displacement of a wave from its equilibrium (rest) position. It represents the strength or intensity of the wave, such as the height of the wave in a sound signal or the peak voltage in an electrical signal.
    In telecommunications, the amplitude of a signal is crucial because it directly impacts the strength and clarity of the transmission.

    • In Audio Signals: Higher amplitudes correspond to louder sounds, while lower amplitudes result in quieter sounds.
    • In Electrical Signals: Higher amplitudes mean stronger signals that can travel further or penetrate better through mediums.

    Decibel

    The decibel (dB) is used to express signal strength, power, and loss in telecommunications, often for gain and attenuation measurements.

    Signal Modulation

    Signal modulation is the process of altering a carrier wave's properties (amplitude, frequency, or phase) to encode information for transmission. Different modulation techniques are employed based on the communication medium and desired signal characteristics.

    Analogic Modulation

    Amplitude Modulation (AM)

    Amplitude Modulation (AM) involves varying the amplitude (strength) of the carrier wave in proportion to the input signal. This technique is commonly used in analog audio broadcasting (e.g., AM radio).

    Frequency Modulation (FM)

    Frequency Modulation (FM) works by varying the frequency of the carrier wave in accordance with the amplitude of the input signal. The frequency changes by small amounts, with the degree of deviation determined by the strength of the signal.

    Phase Modulation (PM)

    Phase Modulation (PM) changes the phase of the carrier signal based on the modulating signal. The phase shift varies according to the instantaneous amplitude of the input signal.

    Bitrate vs Baud Rate

    In telecommunications, bitrate and baud rate are two key metrics that describe data transmission speed, but they refer to different aspects of the signal:

    • Bitrate:
      The bitrate is the number of bits transmitted per second (bps). It reflects the amount of actual data (0s and 1s) sent over a communication channel. For example, a bitrate of 1000 bps means 1000 bits of data are transmitted every second.
    • Baud Rate:
      The baud rate (or symbol rate) is the number of signal changes (symbols) per second. Each symbol may represent one or more bits depending on the modulation technique used.

    Relationship

    Bitrate = Baud Rate × Number of bits per symbol

    • If each symbol carries 1 bit → Bitrate = Baud Rate
    • If each symbol carries 4 bits (e.g., 16-QAM) → Bitrate = 4 × Baud Rate

    Example:

    A 16-QAM signal transmits 4 bits per symbol. If the baud rate is 1,000 symbols/sec, the bitrate is: 1,000 × 4 = 4,000 bps

    Multiplexing Techniques

    Multiplexing is a method used in telecommunications to combine multiple signals into a single transmission channel, allowing efficient use of bandwidth and infrastructure. It enables multiple data streams to share the same physical medium without interfering with each other.

    Types of Multiplexing Techniques

    • Time Division Multiplexing (TDM)
      Each signal is assigned a specific time slot in a repeating sequence. Only one signal transmits at a time, but they alternate rapidly, giving the illusion of simultaneous transmission.
    • Frequency Division Multiplexing (FDM)
      Each signal is transmitted on a different frequency within the available bandwidth. This is common in analog systems like radio and cable TV.
    • Wavelength Division Multiplexing (WDM)
      A form of FDM used in optical fiber communication. Different data channels are sent over distinct light wavelengths (colors) through a single fiber.
    • Code Division Multiple Access (CDMA)
      All signals share the same frequency and time but use unique codes for separation. Common in mobile and satellite communications.
    • Space Division Multiplexing (SDM)
      Separate signals are transmitted in distinct physical paths or channels, such as different wires, fiber cores, or antenna beams.
