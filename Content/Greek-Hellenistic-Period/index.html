    Greek Hellenistic Period

    The Greek Hellenistic Period, lasting from the death of Alexander the Great in 323 BCE to the emergence of the Roman Empire in 31 BCE, marks a significant era in Greek history and culture. This period was characterized by the spread of Greek influence across a vast portion of the ancient world, following Alexander's conquests, which stretched from Greece through Asia Minor, Egypt, Persia, and into India.

    • Start: c. 323 BCE
    • End: 146 BCE

    Timeline

    Key Features of the Greek Hellenistic Period:

    Expansion of Greek Culture

    • Following Alexander the Great’s conquests, Greek culture spread across much of the Mediterranean, Asia Minor, Egypt, and parts of India...
    • This blending of Greek and local traditions formed a new cosmopolitan culture known as Hellenism...
    • Greek language, art, and philosophy flourished and influenced regions as far as Persia and India.

    Political Changes

    • After Alexander’s death, his empire was divided among his generals, the Diadochi, creating several Hellenistic kingdoms...
    • Major kingdoms included the Ptolemaic Kingdom in Egypt, the Seleucid Empire in Mesopotamia and Persia, and the Antigonid dynasty in Macedonia...
    • These kingdoms often clashed with one another, but also shared Greek cultural influences.

    Art and Architecture

    • Hellenistic art moved beyond the idealized forms of the Classical period, focusing on realism, emotion, and movement...
    • Sculptures like the Laocoön Group and the Venus de Milo are iconic examples of Hellenistic artistry...
    • Architecture became more grandiose and complex, with urban planning and monumental buildings in new Hellenistic cities like Alexandria.

    Philosophical Developments

    • New schools of philosophy emerged, focusing on individual happiness and ethics...
    • Stoicism, founded by Zeno of Citium, emphasized virtue, wisdom, and self-control...
    • Epicureanism, founded by Epicurus, advocated for the pursuit of pleasure and the avoidance of pain...
    • Cynicism, led by Diogenes, encouraged living in harmony with nature and rejecting societal conventions.

    Science and Technology

    • The Hellenistic period was a time of significant scientific progress...
    • Archimedes made advances in mathematics, physics, and engineering, including the Archimedean screw...
    • Euclid developed geometry, creating Euclidean geometry that remains foundational to modern mathematics...
    • Eratosthenes calculated the Earth’s circumference with remarkable accuracy...
    • Hellenistic scientists, such as Hipparchus and Aristarchus, made early strides in astronomy.

    Cultural and Intellectual Centers

    • Alexandria in Egypt became the most famous intellectual and cultural hub of the Hellenistic world...
    • The Library of Alexandria housed one of the largest collections of texts from all over the ancient world...
    • The Museum of Alexandria fostered research in various fields, particularly in science, philosophy, and literature.

    Decline and Roman Conquest

    • The Hellenistic kingdoms gradually weakened due to internal conflicts and pressure from rising powers like Rome...
    • The Roman Republic began absorbing the Hellenistic kingdoms, culminating in the defeat of Cleopatra VII and Mark Antony at the Battle of Actium in 31 BCE, which ended the Ptolemaic Kingdom in Egypt...
    • The full integration of the Hellenistic world into the Roman Empire marked the end of the Hellenistic period.

    Scientific and Intellectual Achievements

    • Archimedes made fundamental contributions to mathematics and mechanics, including the principle of buoyancy and lever theory...
    • Euclid's Elements became a cornerstone of geometry...
    • Eratosthenes calculated the Earth's size and created an early system of latitude and longitude...
    • The development of heliocentric theory by Aristarchus was a precursor to later astronomical discoveries.
