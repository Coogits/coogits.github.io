    Phase Transition

    A phase transition (or phase change) is the process in which a substance changes from one state of matter to another, such as from a solid to a liquid, a liquid to a gas, or vice versa. This transformation occurs under specific conditions of temperature and pressure and is characterized by a change in the physical properties and molecular arrangement of the substance.


    ### Types of Phase transition
    • Melting: Solid to liquid (e.g., ice melting into water)
    • Freezing: Liquid to solid (e.g., water freezing into ice)
    • Vaporization: Liquid to gas (e.g., water boiling into steam)
    • Condensation: Gas to liquid (e.g., steam condensing into water)
    • Sublimation: Solid directly to gas (e.g., dry ice sublimating into carbon dioxide gas)
    • Deposition: Gas directly to solid (e.g., frost forming on surfaces)

    Key Points

    • Distinct States:
      The common states of matter involved in phase transitions include solid, liquid, gas, and plasma.

    • Energy Exchange:
      During a phase transition, the substance absorbs or releases energy in the form of latent heat without a change in temperature. For example, when ice melts to form water, it absorbs latent heat of fusion even though its temperature remains constant during the transition.

    • Abrupt Changes in Properties:
      Phase transitions often involve sudden changes in physical properties such as density, specific heat, and molecular structure.
