    Neutron

    A neutron is a neutral subatomic particle found in the nucleus of an atom. Along with protons, neutrons contribute to the majority of an atom’s mass. Neutrons play a key role in stabilizing the nucleus and are involved in nuclear reactions like fission.

    Charge and Mass

    • Charge: Neutral (no charge, symbolized as 0 or n⁰)
    • Mass: Approximately 1.675 × 10⁻²⁷ kg, or 1.008665 amu
    • The mass of a neutron is slightly greater than that of a proton by about 0.1%.

    Role in the Atom

    • Neutrons are located in the nucleus alongside protons, contributing to the overall mass of the atom.
    • The number of neutrons in an atom can vary, even for atoms of the same element. Atoms of the same element with different numbers of neutrons are called isotopes.
    • Neutrons help stabilize the nucleus by reducing the electrostatic repulsion between the positively charged protons.
      • Without neutrons, the nucleus would not be stable due to the repulsive force between protons.

    Internal Structure

    Neutrons, like protons, are composite particles made up of three quarks:

    • Two down quarks (d)
    • One up quark (u) These quarks are held together by the strong nuclear force, mediated by gluons, classifying neutrons as baryons (a type of hadron).

    Stability and Lifespan

    • Neutrons in the nucleus are stable, but a free neutron (not bound in a nucleus) is unstable.
      • A free neutron decays via beta decay into a proton, electron, and antineutrino, with a half-life of about 10 minutes.
      • This process is important in nuclear reactions and the formation of elements in stars.

    Historical Note

    • The neutron was discovered in 1932 by James Chadwick in an experiment that involved bombarding beryllium with alpha particles and detecting the resulting neutral radiation.
    • This discovery was crucial for understanding the structure of atoms and led to advancements in nuclear physics and the development of nuclear energy.
